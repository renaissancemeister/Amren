High-Tech ‘Ears’ Listen for Shots

Cara Buckley, New York Times, November 22, 2009

{snip}

ShotSpotter is made by a 14-year-old eponymous company in Mountain View, Calif., that is considered a leader in the field of acoustic gunshot tracking, though there are other systems: Sentri, made by Safety Dynamics of Tucson, monitors smaller areas with cameras and microphones, and the mobile Boomerang system, made by BBN Technologies of Cambridge, Mass., is used by the military in Afghanistan and Iraq to track sniper fire.

The ShotSpotter system uses microphones that transmit the sound of a gunshot to a police mainframe computer seconds after the firing. The wireless sensors also gauge their distance from the noise, like bats, triangulating the information to direct the police to the shooting site. If the system sounds like Big Brother, it is, kind of — except, one police official insisted, it eavesdrops only on things that go bang.

{snip}

ShotSpotter was deployed in Nassau County in late July, covering three square miles of an area the police refer to as “the gun corridor” in Roosevelt and Uniondale, two struggling, adjoining towns. {snip}

12 — Michael C. Scott wrote at 12:20 PM on November 25:

In regard to Jewamongyou’s speculation about silencers, I offer the following:

Suppressors aren’t quite what Hollywood makes them out to be. The tiny suppressors shown in movies would be useless, as are silencers on revolvers (the gas escaping out the forcing cone between the cylinder and the barrel would still make a nice “bang”).

Effective suppressors can be made, but the average street punk typically doesn’t have the intellect or mechanical skills to pull the job off (how many of them know what “spiral diffusion” is?). Adapting the muffler from a lawnmower engine might reduce the noise of a gunshot if adapted as a pistol silencer, but engine mufflers typically work in part by forcing exhaust gases to slow down by going around corners. This method obviously has its limitations if the high-pressure gas you want to quiet down is accompanied by a fast-moving piece of lead. A 2-liter soda bottle will fit on the end of some self-loading pistols, but this generally involves removing the front sight. The large bottle also so obscures what is left of the sight picture that it makes the firearm so inaccurate that it becomes a close-range proposition no more effective than a baseball bat. I actually tested one on the muzzle of my old Ruger .22” target pistol about 12 years ago. Using .22” CB short (a weak “CB” load in a .22” short case), noise was reduced to approximately the level of a boy’s air rifle, but again, accuracy was nonexistant.

Centerfire rifle cartridges don’t silence well, because the supersonic “crack” caused by the bullet exceeding the sound barrier is unavoidable unless the street thug is also a handloader who can cook up some special low-velocity ammunition for himself.

I don’t think suppressed/moderated/silenced firearms are much of a problem in street crime, and I’ll bet the law-enforcement officers who read or post here have never encountered one in the course of an actual investigation.

To make a long story short, silencers are probably a non-issue.
