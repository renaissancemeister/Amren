Surgeon General: More Minority Doctors Needed

Mike Stobbe, Comcast News, December 3, 2009

The new U.S. Surgeon General on Thursday called for stepped-up efforts in increasing the number of minority physicians.

In what was one of her first speeches to a large crowd since she was sworn in Nov. 3, Dr. Regina Benjamin noted that the proportion of U.S. physicians who are minorities is only 6 percent — the same proportion as a century ago.

“There’s something wrong with that,” said Benjamin, speaking at a conference on health disparities at a hotel in downtown Atlanta.

While many medical schools would love to announce “diversity” by increasing black enrollment - unlike other professional schools - they are far more careful about admitting applicants who cannot succeed.

Admission to US medical schools remains very competitive even for strongly qualified white applicants. Medical schools do not like to play games or pander to students who “need help” for several reasons. First, if the student drops out for academic reasons not only does the attrition look bad for the school it becomes a problem to fill the lost student’s spot as the class moves on to the second, third, and fourth years of study. This creates lost tuition revenue as well.

If the school does manage to push through a weak student then new problems emerge. The newly graduated doctor enters a residency program - does poorly - and the school gets tagged with a bad reputation and hurts future candidates for the residency slots. Many people fail to realize that it is often more important in a doctor’s career where he or she completes a residency than where he or she actually graduated from medical school.

Also, a marginally qualified black candidate will also have issues being surrounded by high achieving white students. To be frank, medical educators - fairly or unfairly - are not known for empathy and no student or resident gets a free pass - they are all overworked and criticized just the same.

If one reads the article above you would think that medical schools are somehow discriminating against black candidates. Not so. Unlike schools of education, for example, where most blacks get doctorates, or even law schools, where academically weak students can pass on the basis of subjectivity in exam grading, simply attending medical school is only part of the equation. Medical students still have to pass parts I, II, and III of national licensing exams (boards) among other exams (such as board certification exams). Medical schools are highly embarrassed if their students fail board exams.

Overall, US medical schools do not tolerate academically undisciplined students. As much as members of the admissions committee wanted to consider accepting even a weak black candidate when I was on faculty (late 1990s) they simply felt it too much of a gamble after being repeatedly burned in the 1980s.

At least for now, American medical education tends not to place too much emphasis on political correctness when it comes to training physicians. Still, black candidates were often often invited for interviews or accepted despite having markedly lower GPAs and MCAT scores than rejected white candidates. I found this to be extremely upsetting and unfair.

The low number of blacks in US medical schools is also due to entrance requirements. For example, law schools often require only a college degree. The student can choose to major in “African Studies” or basket weaving or whatever. US medical schools - while generally open to students in any undergraduate major - also require applicants to take two years of college chemistry, including organic chemistry, one year of biology, one year of physics, and one year of math - at a minimum. Blacks frequently are unable to complete the rquirements - hence they cannot apply.

In science courses - and in medical school - grading is basically objective. In biochemistry or gross anatomy or pharmacology there is one right answer. No one can claim racism.

I graduated from medical school in the late 1980s. The graduating class numbered 141. There were six black students who started - four years later two graduated. The other four were dismissed for academic reasons. Three non-blacks who started did not graduate - non for academic reasons.

My point is - to increase black enrollment the academic and educational standards will have to be lowered. There is no other way. This turns another field of excellence into a bastion of failure - it is a total disgrace. Still, the study of medicine is a lot of work - if nothing else medical students cannot be lazy. I’d like to see how that will be changed.

Lastly, our friend the surgeon general attended Morehouse School of Medicine in Atlanta. This “historically black” school accepts mostly black students. While the school clearly discriminates against non-blacks based on academics everyone basically looks the other way because black doctors have to come from somewhere.

Based on the AMA’s statistics (the AMA loves statistics) via AMCAS (the application service) for 2008, the average MCAT score of an admitted Morehouse student was 27 (vs the national average of 32). Science GPAs fell lower than 2.7. The other two “historically black” med schools were worse - Meharry in Tennessee had an average entering MCAT score of 25 and Howard, in DC, was also 25. If not for these “black schools” there would be virtually no black physicians in the US since over 50% of practicing black doctors in the US graduated from one of these institutions.
