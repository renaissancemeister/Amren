You, Too, Can Own a $100 Trillion Note — From Zimbabwe

Amanda Hamon, Journal and Courier (West Lafayette, Indiana), December 30, 2009

{snip}

Store owner Randy Ramsey said he recently bought 60 Zimbabwean $100 trillion notes — complete with colorful artwork — to add to the store’s wide collection of foreign and out-of-circulation currency.

The notes were available for purchase Tuesday, at $10 a bill.

“We had bought this as a novelty piece, and to show what inflation does,” said floor manager Chad Ramsey. “A hundred trillion is a really hard number to comprehend.”

The notes have been out of circulation in their African home country since June, Chad Ramsey said.

Before then, they were worth about 33 cents American.

In Zimbabwe, $100 trillion is about enough to buy three eggs, said sales associate Zach Briggs, who researched the currency.

The outrageous inflation suggested in that scenario makes the notes a lesson in economics, store workers said. Zimbabwe is home to one of the worst hyperinflation crises in the world thanks to an economy flushed with printed currency.

{snip}

The $100 trillion notes are the third version of that Zimbabwean denomination, the Ramseys said.

The country’s current $100 trillion bill was reprinted in June to eliminate some of its dizzying zeroes.

{snip}

14 — Michael C. Scott wrote at 4:30 PM on January 5:

Correct, John. The hyperinflation in Weimar Germany was grotesque. I have old Weimar postage stamps, and on of them was originally printed at a “50 Millionen” (50 million) Mark denomination, but was re-stamped before it could be issued as “5 Milliard” (5 billion). Keep in mind when thinking about this that a European billion is not the 1000 million used in the United States, but instead is one million million, so under US numerology, this postage stamp is 5 times ten-to-the-twelth Marks, or what we would call 5 trillion.

The reason for the runaway inflation in Germany after world war one was that the victorious allies demanded Germany pay the entire cost of the war. Germany was just as bankrupt as the rest of the parties to the conflict were, and so paid by simply printing worthless money. I do not know whether German currency was backed at the time by their government’s holdings of precious metals, or whether - like today’s US dollar - it was simply backed by the value of the German economy (all the goods and services produced in Germany each year), but it doesn’t really matter. Printing more banknotes simply dilutes the value of what is already in circulation (and is effectively an invisible form of taxation, as governments, by printing this money, are stealing some of the value of privately-held cash and bank deposits.)
