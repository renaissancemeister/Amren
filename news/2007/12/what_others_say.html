Charles Onyango-Obbo, Daily Nation (Nairobi), Dec. 27, 2007

THERE IS OLD SCHOOL, there’s the African Big Man … and then there’s Comrade Jacob Gedleyihiekisa Zuma, who last week won the presidency of South Africa’s ruling African National Congress (ANC).

The boisterous and populist leader’s reputation took a beating last year when he was charged with raping an HIV positive family friend. He was acquitted.

Having wrested the leadership of the ANC from Thabo Mbeki, unless he is convicted of corruption given that fresh charges are being brought against him, Zuma is all but assured of becoming South Africa’s president in 2009.

With that prospect looming, far-sighted observers are already contemplating what The Times (London) called a “pressing problem”. That is, which of Zuma’s many wives, not to mention mistresses, would be the First Lady?

An “unashamed Zulu traditionalist” (according to the Times), Zuma is quite happy to admit publicly that he is a polygamist.

He has recognised at least 18 children from “four official” marriages and several girlfriends. Two children have been born to him since his acquittal in the rape trial.

Zuma still does like some of our more old-fashioned fathers used to do. He has a “village wife”, Sizakele Khumalo. She is the presumed “No 1”, and lives at his homestead in Nklanda, Kwazulu-Natal. He has no children with her.

The “city wife” is Mantuli Zuma, who married the big man five years ago. They have two children. Then there was Kabe Zuma. They had five children. She committed suicide in 2000.

Diamini Zuma couldn’t take his ways much longer. They divorced in 1998. They have four daughters. She is now Foreign minister and, not surprisingly, a Mbeki ally.

He has a true town wife in the person of Thobeca Stacy Mabhija, with whom he has two children. With a name like that, small wonder she works at a mobile phone company. The Times describes her as an elegant woman known for her designer outfits.

As with very many men, there is an old-flame, Minah Shongwe, in the frame. She has a son with Zuma.

Comrade Zuma, by the way, is 65. Truly impressive that he still has so much energy at that age.

In South Africa’s The Herald, Mandla Seleoane has a column dated today (note the reference to Eugene Valberg, speaker at the upcoming AR conference):

“Maybe Our Critics Were Right About the Roads.”

“SOME time in the mid-‘80s, one Professor Eugene Valberg from the United States wrote a mini philosophical treatise on why black people in South Africa deserved to be oppressed and denied political rights….He could not trust black people to manage a country, as he said, with five-lane roads, and feared that we might mess up a promising economy.

I wrote, then, a response to the good professor and debated not only what I saw as flawed reasoning processes in his work, but indeed challenged the audacity of an American to think that he could lecture anyone on democracy…

I would also argue that, on the whole, we have not destroyed a promising economy, but that we seem to have brought it to higher levels than the Nats would ever hope to.

However there is one aspect I would concede to Valberg and it is that we have made a mess of our roads.

I would concede to him that, the way we behave, it would appear indeed that we are not equipped to handle five-lane roads. The sort of things that one sees on our roads post-1994 are absolutely crazy and it is hard to understand why our government seems reluctant or unable to correct the situation.

The other day I was driving out of Silverton in the direction of Pretoria and there was some congestion at the intersection of Pretoria Road and Kraanvoël Street.

I thought that maybe there was an accident, but lo and behold, some fun-loving car-load of compatriots had parked in the middle of the road and were enjoying meat and drinks.

Everyone had to navigate their cars carefully past the party, whilst the revellers went about their pleasure nonchalantly.

When I learned to drive, and it was under the Nats, there were things I was taught you simply didn’t do on the road.

I was taught you never operated a car on a public road if it emitted excessive smoke……

Now, all of these were good rules even though they were made by a system that we disliked intensely…

Under the Nats we just knew that there would be hell to pay if we did not observe them.

Yet how often do you come across a car that all but smokes you to death, driven with complete impunity on our roads today?

How often do you find people pushing a car on the freeway, and not really trying to get it off the road, but trying to reach some destination that way?…

So yes, I would give it to Valberg that we don’t know how to behave on the sophisticated network of roads that we have and that our government does not appear to have a plan to get us to behave appropriately.

I would also concede that we have messed quite a few of those roads up as well, judging by the potholes that appear to be becoming a kind of permanent feature of our roads.”
