James C. McKinley Jr., New York Times, Feb. 10

MEXICO CITY — Since an aide to President Vicente Fox was arrested last week on suspicion of having contacts with drug traffickers, Mexico has been unsettled by the prospect that the tentacles of its criminal networks extend to the highest levels of government.

Though prosecutors have not publicly identified the cartel involved, law enforcement officials say the aide appeared to be in contact with members of a syndicate run by Mexico’s most wanted man: Joaquín Guzmán Loera.

After escaping from a maximum security prison four years ago, Guzmán, 50, has proved himself to be one of the most clever, aggressive and ruthless gangsters Mexico has ever produced.

He is also one so feared and respected in his hometown, Badiraguato, in the northwestern state of Sinoloa, that the newly elected mayor there will not even say his name aloud to a reporter.

The federal agents who have tried to hunt him down have missed again and again as he has slipped away just before they arrived, apparently tipped off by local people. Sinoloan musicians make up ballads about his exploits. One high-ranking Mexican law enforcement official recently called him “un zorro,” or a fox.

Yet Guzmán, who is known as El Chapo, or Shorty, is no Zorro, nor a Robin Hood. Law enforcement officials on both sides of the Rio Grande say he has almost single-handedly ignited the underworld war that is sweeping Mexico and has caused the death of dozens of drug traffickers, police officers, federal agents and bystanders in recent months.

Mexican Tribes Forced To Grow Crops for Drug Gangs

Reuters, Feb. 9

MONTERREY, Mexico — Mexican drug gangs are forcing Indian tribes to abandon their traditional crops and grow marijuana and heroin poppies, according to a study released yesterday.

The report by Mexico’s National Anthropology and History Institute said armed drug gangs were driving communities of Tarahumara, Guarijio and Pima Indians to give up their age-old way of life in the mountains of Sonora and Chihuahua states.

Researchers said drug gangs from neighboring Sinaloa state are entering the remote region and forcing the native villagers to stop growing traditional corn in favor of marijuana and heroin poppies.
