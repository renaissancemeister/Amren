Nigeria Leader Takes Reins In Troubled State

Violence prompts emergency decree

Dulue Mbachu and Glenn McKenzie, AP, Chic. Tribune, May 19

LAGOS, Nigeria — Nigeria’s president declared a state of emergency in a troubled central state Tuesday, invoking sweeping powers in a bid to halt religious and ethnic bloodletting that he said posed a “great threat” to national unity.

President Olusegun Obasanjo sacked Gov. Joshua Dariye and dissolved the legislature in the central state of Plateau, saying elected officials had “wittingly and unwittingly encouraged acts that have subverted peace and tranquility.” The declaration — made in an address on national radio and television — came after months of violence in the state.

Retired Maj. Gen. Chris Ali, who served in the Nigerian army under previous junta rulers, will replace Dariye.

Dariye’s spokesman, Stanley Bentu, said the governor accepted Obasanjo’s declaration.

First emergency order

It was Obasanjo’s first emergency declaration — giving him the ability to govern by decree — since his 1999 election ended 15 years of repressive military rule. Since then, intertwined ethnic, political and religious violence has left more than 10,000 dead across Africa’s most populous nation.

The move gives Obasanjo the ability to govern the state without consulting other officials or the national legislature. Plateau is patrolled by armed police and soldiers, recently accused by Human Rights Watch of indiscriminate killings.

Attacks against Muslims by Christian ethnic groups in Plateau killed hundreds this month, sent tens of thousands of refugees fleeing and prompted mob revenge attacks against Christians in Kano state, where scores more died.

Resurgent fighting in Plateau “constitutes a grave threat to law and order and a great danger to security in Plateau state and neighboring states,” Obasanjo said. “It is therefore clearly a great threat to the security and unity of Nigeria.”

Fighting has plagued Plateau since September 2001, when more than 1,000 were killed in the previously tranquil city of Jos, 210 miles northeast of the capital, Abuja.

Bloodshed surged again this month with the killings of hundreds of Hausa-speaking Muslims by members of a Christian Tarok-speaking ethnic militia in the town of Yelwa. Violence spread last week as Muslim Hausas rioted in the northern city of Kano, attacking Christians and killing scores in apparent revenge for the Yelwa slayings.

“We need to take serious steps to stem the tide of what has become a near-mutual genocide in the affected areas of Plateau state,” Obasanjo said.

Christian and Muslim groups had both accused Dariye of siding against them in the violence. However, Obasanjo’s declaration pleased neither group as religious leaders and human-rights activists accused Obasanjo of exceeding his powers.

Saidu Dogo, a regional official of Nigeria’s main Christian umbrella group in the northern city of Kaduna, accused Obasanjo of making Plateau’s Christian-led government a scapegoat.

Lateef Adegbite, secretary general of Nigeria’s Supreme Council for Islamic Affairs, said he approved of the emergency powers but disagreed with the appointment of a general, citing Muslim fears that Obasanjo, a former general, was “militarizing the country.”

’Great danger’ for democracy

Gani Fawehinmi, a human-rights campaigner, argued that Obasanjo had “no right” under the 1999 constitution to dismiss state officials.

“What Obasanjo has done portends great danger for our democracy and will scare away foreign investors,” he said. “The sustenance of our democracy is hanging in the balance.”

Obasanjo did not say whether Tuesday’s emergency declaration was linked to a recent security force investigation of unspecified “security breaches” some army commanders described as a plot by army officers and civilian collaborators to topple the president in a coup.

The “security breaches” prompted government officials to disallow anti-government demonstrations, with the president’s spokeswoman Remi Oyo saying any protester would be deemed an “enemy of the state.”

In 1979, Obasanjo became Nigeria’s first junta leader to voluntarily step aside. He returned to politics two decades later as an elected civilian. He was re-elected last year in a vote rejected as rigged by his main opponent, former military ruler Muhammadu Buhari.
