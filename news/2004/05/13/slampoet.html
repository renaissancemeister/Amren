Slam Poet Stirs Up The U.S. Melting Pot

Jack Zink, Sun-Sentinel.com, May 12

New York — Sarah Jones is a slam poet who has collected the voices within her head and turned them into a repertory company. Her current one-woman show, Bridge & Tunnel, is a roller coaster of comedy, poignancy and drama.

The off-Broadway showcase at a 300-seat downtown theater is her fourth full-length monologue in a string that’s generated a buzz from San Francisco to Washington,s D.C. Not since Lily Tomlin has a performer so compellingly depicted the ingredients of America’s melting pot.

Jones’ subjects are all immigrants in contemporary New York, telling us of their dreams and frustrations at an annual poetry reading organized by a good-natured Pakistani emcee. Tony Taccone directs, which must be like sitting on a buckboard holding the reins of an unbroken stallion as Jones whips through more than a dozen very different characters.

The stage is a 12-inch-high platform at one end of a cafeteria-sized venue, facing an audience on stack chairs loaded onto risers. Jones works the ringsiders and, at one point, takes a seat in the middle of the room to portray an elderly Russian crank.

The setting is a café in South Queens. A homeless black woman is the usher who delivers the opening speech about candy wrappers and cell phone behavior, with a few asides about the economic divide she’s facing. An elderly Polish Jew remembers how her people were branded outsiders and troublemakers, the same as the Hispanics she’s talking to today.

A young Vietnamese-American spews frustration and rage at the WASP elite, while a Mexican who lost his legs pines over an even greater tragedy: the fiancée who died while trying to cross the border.

Emcee Mohammed Ali is an accountant and a 27-year U.S. resident, with the delusion that he’s a polished host. In between his awkward introductions, he’s on the phone worried about the immigration service’s sudden interest in him and his wife.

There are no specific connecting links in their stories, but Jones’ choice of topical material builds a vivid panorama of the cultural pot in mid-melt. In the face of constant harangues that the monodrama is a dead-end art form, artists like her keep finding ways to prove its vitality.
