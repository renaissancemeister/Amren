Ex-Boyfriend Kills Stanwood Teen, Himself, Shocking Small Community

Deborah Bach, Gregory Roberts, and Sam Skolnik, Seattle Post-Intelligencer, May 26

STANWOOD — The romance was over, but Mainor “Mario” Valentin wouldn’t stay away.

For two months, Valentin tracked his ex-girlfriend, Stanwood High School senior Dayna Fure, all over town — to her home, to her grandparents’ house, even to her sister’s house in Marysville.

On May 13, he followed her to a nail salon and then to the Stanwood retirement home where she worked, showing her a gun he had bought and telling her he was going to kill himself.

The next day — her 18th birthday — Fure got a court order to keep Valentin at a distance.

It didn’t work.

On Monday, Valentin — a 23-year-old would-be policeman — shot Fure in the head and then killed himself the same way, authorities said. Her father found the bodies in his daughter’s bedroom when he returned home about 5 p.m.

The murder-suicide sent shock waves through this small Snohomish County community, where Fure’s family has lived for many years, and left a high school in mourning.

“I couldn’t believe it when I heard,” said Stanwood High junior Rachael Lipinski, who was on the cross-country team with Fure. “I thought it couldn’t be true.”

“Everybody’s devastated,” said John Gilday, a restaurant designer and head of Waterfund, a Kirkland charitable organization that builds drinking-water cisterns and schools for the poor in Mexico and provides health services there.

Gilday met Fure when she and a friend volunteered to help build a school in Mexico in 2002. Fure, he said, was “bubbly” and “determined,” “fearless” and “honest.”

“She was everything that I would want my daughter to be, if I had a daughter,” Gilday said.

Fure brought Valentin with her to an orientation session before she went to Mexico. Valentin glared in silence through the hourlong presentation, Gilday recalled.

Later, Gilday said, Fure told him that Valentin didn’t want her to go to Mexico — and didn’t like her to do anything without him.

Valentin’s behavior turned more ominous after Fure attempted to break off their relationship two months ago, according to the Snohomish County Sheriff’s Office and statements she made to obtain the protection order.

He followed her, called her often and wrote letters threatening suicide, Fure said. “I have given many reasons and hints to leave me alone and after two months of separation, he is determined to do whatever to get me back,” she wrote.

On May 13, the sheriff’s office said, he trailed her to the nail salon and displayed a small handgun, again threatening to kill himself. Fure left the salon for her job in the dining room at a Stanwood retirement home.

When Fure got off work later that day, she found Valentin inside her car, she said. She called 911. Police came, and after a 90-minute negotiation via cellular phone, took Valentin into custody and then to the Colby Campus of Providence Everett Medical Center for a mental-health evaluation.

Police discovered a gun in his car and confiscated it. The next day, Fure got the protection order. And sometime before Monday, Valentin got another gun.

It was unclear yesterday how long Valentin was at the hospital before being released. A hospital spokeswoman did not return calls.

People with potential mental health problems deemed to be a threat to themselves or others can be committed involuntarily for up to 72 hours, or longer after a court hearing.

Valentin, who moved to Seattle from Honduras with his family in 1992, worked as a downtown safety “ambassador” and was applying to become a Seattle police officer, according to one of his sisters.

Marta Valentin, 27, said her younger brother met Fure at a nightclub in Seattle and that the two had been dating off and on for two years.

“We loved her. Oh my God, she had such spirit. We’re going to miss her,” she said last night at the modest, two-story townhouse in West Seattle’s High Point neighborhood where her brother lived with her, their mother and two other sisters.

Marta Valentin described her brother as a “great kid” who helped out other neighborhood youths, but said he’d been depressed about Fure.

“She was beautiful,” Marta said, crying. “She wanted to do so much with her life. … We can’t believe this happened.”

Yesterday, students and staff at Stanwood High School grieved the loss of the popular senior.

Outside the building, the U.S. flag flew at half-staff above a memorial where students placed flowers, candles and balloons. A simple wooden cross was propped up next to a potted sunflower, and a drawing of a smiling Fure was taped to the flagpole above photos of her as a toddler and at the recent school prom.

Messages chalked on the sidewalk and written on a banner mourned the loss of a girl described as always optimistic.

Lipinski remembered being on a long run with her last fall when she pulled up lame. Fure asked Lipinski how she was doing and encouraged her to keep going.

“She was like, ‘We can make it. We’ve only got a couple miles left,’ “ Lipinski remembered.

Teammate Stacy Holtmann said Fure could always be counted on to cheer on others. “At races, people would always cry if they did bad,” she said. “Not Dayna — she’d say, ‘You tried. That’s all that matters.’ “

Overcome with emotion, senior Sierra Webb said: “She was an angel — that’s all I can say.”

Fure attended Stanwood-Camano schools since kindergarten, and was known as a diligent student and a talented athlete. She was ranked 38th in this year’s senior class of 366 students, and maintained a grade-point average of 3.8. Fure lettered for four years in cross-country running, and also had perfect attendance on the team during that time.

During this school year’s track season, she received a “best attitude” award.

Vice Principal Jim Piccolo, who has known Fure’s family for nearly three decades, said she’d been accepted to Gonzaga University and was looking forward to starting there in the fall.

“That was a real dream of hers, to be there,” he said. “When she was accepted, it was a breath of fresh air for her.”

Family members declined to comment yesterday. Fure’s friends said she was extremely close to her older sister, Danielle, who was on the cross-country team with her before graduating from Stanwood High two years ago. Fure reportedly also had two stepbrothers.

Lynda Wagner, general manager of the Merrill Gardens retirement center, said Fure was “a doll” who loved the residents and made each one feel special.

“She was very, very giving of herself to the people she took care of.”
