Eight-Year-Olds Becoming Hooked On ‘Tik’

Erica Perez, iol (SA), Jun. 17

“When you look at how many kids are doing drugs now — that’s our future labour force. It’s everybody’s problem, it affects all of us.”

This is the chilling assessment of John Malcolm, of the Fish Hoek Drug Crisis Centre, who has been involved in the drug culture for 28 years — as an addict, in recovery and now as a drug counsellor.

The popularity of speed, a drug known as methamphetamine or “tik”, has exploded in the past year, making the drug problem the worst the Western Cape has ever seen.

‘I get up to five calls a day on various kids’

“I have never seen anything like it,” said Malcolm.

“In some communities, you are more likely to find youngsters who are using speed than not using. Kids as young as eight and nine are methamphetamine addicts.”

Malcolm said speed was cheap and readily accessible.

Internet recipes make it easy to produce.

“It costs R10 a hit. A ‘straw’ of speed costs anywhere from R50 to R100. The average straw gives you between five and eight hits.”

‘We’re giving birth to our first generation of heroin addicts’

“In the 90s, there were maybe four or five big drug syndicates working Wynberg and Cape Town. Now there are over 200 big drug syndicates.”

Malcolm said his centre, run by just two counsellors, sees two to six families a week, and sometimes handles more than 100 calls.

Chris Missing, a drug counsellor at United Christians Against Narcotics in Durbanville, agreed with Malcolm “one hundred percent”.

“I started counselling just three years ago, when I’d see on average one or two kids a day. This year I get up to five calls a day on various kids. That’s how it’s grown.”

“In all my working time, I’ve never worked as hard as I’m working now. You can hardly deal with one case before the next one’s calling you.”

Methamphetamine in its many forms is a particularly dangerous drug.

“We call it the Jekyl and Hyde drug because of the radical and aggressive personality changes abusers go through,” Malcolm said. “Families and communities are being ripped apart by it.”

In addition, said Missing, many among the youth are abusing more than one drug. Many have begun to use heroin in addition to methamphetamine. The mixture causes them to become “psychotic”.

Some have to be sent for psychiatric treatment in addition to rehabilitation.

Malcolm said the emergence of heroin was deeply troubling.

“We’ve got a heroin problem taking root in this country. We’re giving birth to our first generation of heroin addicts. Once that’s firmly entrenched, it’s going to blow this country apart, unless we start taking steps now.”

The consequences of the drug pandemic are far-reaching, Malcolm said.

“There’s the crime that’s coming off it, plus it’s increasing the risk of HIV and Aids because of the promiscuity within the drug culture — there’s so many things.”
