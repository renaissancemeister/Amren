Court: Whites Should Not Face More Obstacles In Race Discrimination Suits

Detroit Free Press, Jun. 11

LANSING, Mich. (AP) — A white officer who said the Battle Creek Police Department racially discriminated against him should not have a tougher time proving his case in court than a minority employee, the Michigan Supreme Court ruled Friday.

In a 5-2 decision, the court struck down a legal standard that distinguishes between plaintiffs on the basis of race.

The lawsuit was filed by Michael Lind, a white officer who alleged reverse discrimination occurred when the Battle Creek Police Department promoted a black officer who scored lower on written and oral exams than Lind.

The trial court and Michigan Court of Appeals ruled against Lind, who now works as an assistant prosecutor for Calhoun County. Michigan’s lower courts had used a legal test that required a person claiming reverse discrimination to prove that the employer has an unusual background of discriminating against majority groups.

But the state’s highest court overturned those decisions.

The Michigan Civil Rights Act “draws no distinction between individual plaintiffs on account of race,” Justice Stephen Markman wrote for the majority.

The decision pleased Lind’s attorney, Marshall Grate.

“We all ought to stand equally before the law,” Grate said.

But the court’s decision drew sharp rebukes from dissenting Justices Michael Cavanagh and Marilyn Kelly.

“In our society, demeaning acts of prejudice directed against whites because of their race are uncommon,” Kelly wrote in arguing that distinctions based on race are justified.

But Robert Young, the court’s lone black justice, questioned what he called the dissenters’ “Orwellian racial policy preferences” and argued that no citizen should be more equal than another.

The court returned Lind’s case to the trial court for reconsideration. His case had been dismissed by the circuit court.

Glen Lenhoff, a Flint employment discrimination expert, said the ruling could pave the way for challenges in state courts to the University of Michigan law school’s admissions policy, which allows race to be considered under limited circumstances.

The U.S. Supreme Court last year struck down the university’s undergraduate admissions policy that awarded points to racial minorities, but allowed the law school’s more subjective admissions policy to stand.

Lenhoff called the ruling the Michigan Supreme Court’s most “socially significant” in years.

“The Michigan judiciary has made it crystal clear that it will not allow reverse discrimination,” Lenhoff said. “I don’t see how (the court) could allow reverse discrimination in college admissions after this opinion.”

While Lind’s case deals specifically with hiring practices, Lenhoff said the ruling could be brought to bear on higher education because the Michigan Civil Rights Act also affects universities.

Lind brought the suit after unsuccessfully seeking a promotion to sergeant in 1996.

The city’s collective bargaining agreement says that, when a position opens, the city can select any one of its top five candidates. Lind ranked second based on oral and written exams and seniority. The department hired Arthur McClenney, a black patrol officer who ranked fifth.

City Attorney Clyde Robinson said Friday that former Police Chief Tom Pope hired McClenney because he liked his “maturity and sense of duty to the department.”

Lind “came across with the attitude that he felt the position was owed to him,” Robinson said. He added that an Asian-American had ranked first, but also didn’t get the promotion.

Lind could not be reached for comment.

The city is disappointed that the justices got rid of the old standard without putting a new one in its place, Robinson said.

“What are you putting in place? Give us some guidance,” he said.
