Bronx Cheer For Southie: New York Delegation: It’s Too Racist For Us

David R. Guarino, bostonherald.com, Jun. 11

New York Democrats — after mistaking South Boston’s historic L Street Bathhouse for a gay club — have demanded their official convention party be moved out of the neighborhood entirely because of its “racial” past.

Thirty years after the forced busing crisis rocked South Boston, New York’s Democratic Party chief told Boston officials the neighborhood isn’t good enough for the Empire State’s Democratic National Convention reception.

“I am deeply concerned about the selection of South Boston as the reception site given its history of racial turmoil and tension,” Herman Farrell Jr., the New York party boss, wrote in an April letter to organizers, obtained yesterday by the Herald.

Farrell, who is black, said, “For those of us who were on the frontlines of (the civil rights) struggle, the prospect of celebrating in a neighborhood that so fiercely opposed integration is very troubling.”

The letter had Southie pols fuming — even as New York delegation officials yesterday grudgingly accepted South Boston as a reception site.

“It’s obviously written by someone who is ignorant,” said South Boston City Councilor James M. Kelly.

Southie native Michael F. Flaherty, a city councilor at large, said, “They can always bring their resident pit bull Don Zimmer to protect them, if they feel that’s necessary.”

Zimmer, a former New York Yankees coach, went after Red Sox pitcher Pedro Martinez in a brawl during last year’s playoffs.

New York officials, after being contacted by the Herald, suddenly backed off their flat-out refusal to be hosted in South Boston.

“Those were concerns,” said Chung Seto, executive director of the New York Democratic Party. “We have been shown that South Boston is a new Boston and, therefore, the case is resolved.”

During the July DNC, the city host committee — Boston 2004 — will put on a party for each state delegation. To foster citywide spirit, Mayor Thomas M. Menino decided to place the parties in different neighborhoods.

New York was assigned the Curley Recreation Center, known commonly to locals as the old L Street Bathhouse.

Last week, a gossip column in the New York Post reported delegates weren’t happy because in the 1980s in New York, bathhouses were notorious gay clubs.

Sources in City Hall and at Boston 2004 said they believe New Yorkers were mostly miffed they didn’t land one of the choice party spots — like the John F. Kennedy Library or the Museum of Fine Arts. They said the reported homophobic and racial concerns were just a smokescreen.

“Out of all the sites we chose, this was one of the best,” said Julie Burns, director of Boston 2004. “If they still have concerns when they come to the event, all their concerns will be allayed.”

Burns said the party, moved last week allegedly because costs were too high, now likely will be held at the Curley Center after all.

Menino’s director of neighborhood development, Michael Kineavy, said anyone who has visited South Boston knows it’s “open to all.”

“Mr. Farrell obviously hasn’t visited South Boston in a long time, if ever. If he had, he would find a very welcoming environment,” Kineavy said.
