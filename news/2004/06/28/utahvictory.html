Major Victory (Really) In Utah For Immigration Realists

ProjectUSA, Jun. 25

Republican primary voters in Utah’s third congressional district handed supporters of a more moderate and democratic immigration policy a major victory Tuesday when entrenched Congressman Chris Cannon, whose support for amnesties for illegal aliens was by far the dominant issue in the race, could only manage a pathetic 58% of the vote to hold off challenger Matt Throckmorton.

By all the conventional wisdom, Throckmorton should have been shellacked.

Washington lobbyists and special interests with a financial stake in immigration policy poured money into Cannon’s campaign giving Cannon an enormous advantage in the all-important category of campaign funding.

Augmenting Cannon’s huge cash advantage, the media establishment in Utah aggressively supported Cannon — even refusing to call Cannon on the most outrageous lies and misleading advertising.

Predictably, some in Utah are naively describing Cannon’s poor showing as a great victory and a popular endorsement of Cannon’s anti-borders policies. It is emphatically neither.

That the immigration issue even forced Chris Cannon into a primary in the first place is evidence that the issue is potent, but the fact that there was only a 16 percent difference between Throckmorton and Cannon when the voting was over is really a huge indictment of Cannon, as the people who watch such things will be well aware.

Political scientists define a primary race in which the winning margin is less than 20 percent as a competitive race. According to an April 2000 study (out of Brigham Young University, ironically), only 1.2 percent of incumbent primaries in non-redistricting years are competitive.

By coming within 16 percent, Matt Throckmorton took on the incumbent, the financial power of the corporate interests, and a shamelessly hostile local media, and, on the power of the immigration issue, brought Chris Cannon, statistically speaking, to his knees.

While a loss is never easy, Matt Throckmorton should remember that he fought an honorable fight against nearly impossible odds, and made a spectacular showing in the face of American politics at its ugliest. At 36, his political future is very bright.

On the other hand, immigration moderates should know that in Washington, the real target of our work in Utah, Tuesday’s results will be seen for what they are: a major win. And the game is far from over.
