Texas A&M Works to Improve Diversity on Campus

Jeff Brady, WFAA-TV (TX), Apr. 12

Texas A&M has long been criticized for its lack of racial diversity.

Lawmakers have threatened to punish the school financially if things don’t change, so the school has launched an aggressive outreach to develop diversity.

A select group of seniors at Skyline High School received more than $200,000 in scholarship offers to attend A&M. They are minority student-scholars — most of whom have not yet decided where they will go after graduation.

“If I had to decide today, I am leaning toward Rice University,” said Skyline senior Angel Brown.

“If I had to choose today, it would be UT,” said senior Jaime Martinez.

All of the seniors at a recent assembly have already been admitted, however, to Texas A&M. The challenge for the second-largest school in Texas is now to get those students to commit to enrolling this fall, at a campus where only about 2.5 percent of the undergraduates are black, and nine percent are Hispanic.

That compares to three and ten percent at Texas Tech, three and fourteen percent at UT Austin, and 15 and 20 percent minority enrollments, respectively, at the University of Houston.

It’s a diversity disadvantage the Aggies are trying to change…

“There are no quotas anywhere, and the question is whether you take race specifically into account in admissions,” said Texas A&M President Robert Gates. “Frankly, we want to admit kids because of who they are, not what they are.”

The A&M effort includes unique welcomes and tours for minority applicants who visit the campus, along with former students speaking to potential new students during Gates’ high-school visits.

“We’ve tried to make sure that students understand that if they come to Texas A&M, that they find family,” said Texas A&M presidential chief of staff Rodney McLendon.
