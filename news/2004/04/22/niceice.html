ICE Officials Frustrated With ‘Bad Guy’ Image

Kristin Tillotson, startribune.com (Minneapolis), Apr. 22

Immigration and Customs Enforcement (ICE) agents say they are frustrated at being portrayed as bad guys, when they often go out of their way to be generous to potential deportees.

“We get painted in this bad light, but in reality our role is much more supportive,” said Scott Baniecke, field office director for the regional office in Bloomington, which oversees Minnesota, Iowa, North Dakota, South Dakota and Nebraska. “We get thank-you notes from people who have been deported, thanking us for treating them kindly.”

The legal terminology for deportation is now “removal.” Congress in 1996 combined two procedures, deportation and exclusion. The number of people in the five-state area who have been found in violation of the law and in the removal process is about 13,000.

Baniecke and ICE spokesman Tim Counts cite instances in which they have stretched legal limits to accommodate a deportee’s circumstances — such as releasing an eight-months-pregnant woman on her own recognizance, and giving people extra time to get their affairs in order.

Too often, he said, people forget that ICE agents are the enforcers, not the lawmakers.

“There’s a perception that we have a huge amount of leeway in who we deport and who we don’t depending on our mood,” he said. “But we are carrying out the orders of a judge, because we’re obligated to. Every person who is removed has had full access to the due process of the law.”

The Bloomingotn ICE office also offers each detainee a chance to sit down with a lawyer before seeing a judge.

“If you’re going to get arrested for violating immigration law, do it here, because this is a strong pro bono state,” Baniecke said.

More than 75 percent of ICE arrests involve criminal behavior, Counts said. “In reality, we don’t have the time or resources to take on some of these noncriminal cases, but we have to abide by the courts’ decisions.”
