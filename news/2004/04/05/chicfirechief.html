Daley Chooses Trotter to Deflect Political Heat

John Kass, Chic. Tribune, Apr. 2

With racial controversies smearing his Fire Department, Mayor Richard Daley sent his fire commissioner, a white man, into retirement.

Fire Commissioner James Joyce said he was excited to retire. They’re always excited on the way out.

Daley replaced him Thursday with Cortez Trotter, a black man.

Trotter spent his career on the paramedic side of the department, not the firefighting side. So history is being made on two fronts, not just the obvious one.

Yes, Trotter is the first black man to run an overwhelmingly white department, where some firefighters won’t like him because his skin is darker than theirs. They might be some of the same idiots who’ve gone on fire radios recently, anonymously yelling racial slurs.

But what about the other historical front?

Oddly, the new fire commissioner might not be held responsible for fighting fires and probably won’t ever take command of a major fire.

As a paramedic and bureaucrat, he has never spent much time directing firefighters in front of burning buildings.

Yet the mayor insisted that his decision had nothing to do with politics or symbolism.

“No, no no,” Daley said. “It’s not a symbol. He’s qualified. He’s very proud. He’s African-American. . . . But he’s qualified.”

To prove that racial politics have nothing to do with his promotion, Trotter served notice that diversity is the most important issue.

“In order for every community to feel confident in the Fire Department and EMS [ambulance] service they receive, our department must reconcile our operational issues,” Trotter said sternly.

“But more importantly, we must address our human relations and diversity problems.”

He also warned white firefighters who might have a problem with a black boss. He said he wouldn’t tolerate any shenanigans.

“Let me serve notice to those who wrongfully believe that the department is a haven for small-mindedness, offensive behavior and stagnation,” Trotter said.

“We are entering a new era for the Chicago Fire Department. Please recognize that for what it is, or be prepared to face the consequences of your actions.”

For too long, the Chicago Fire Department has been a club for white guys, with the top jobs handed from grandfather to father to son.

That doesn’t work anymore in a city that is mostly black and Latino.

It especially doesn’t work when the city is run by a white mayor-for-life.

But make no mistake, this appointment is about covering Daley’s flank, not the Fire Department’s flank.

Though Daley controls minority politicians, he’s got a problem coming.

This summer, Daley’s friends, the Outfit-connected Duffs, will go on trial in federal court. A grand jury has charged that they fraudulently received $100 million in affirmative action contracts, though they were white guys.

In effect, they stole $100 million from black, Latino and female-owned firms. And I don’t know how anyone can believe that $100 million in deals leaves City Hall without Daley’s OK.

So having Trotter and other minorities out front in high profile positions, getting TV time, can’t hurt the mayor. It can only help.

Trotter, executive director of the Office of Emergency Management and Communications, faces other political challenges too. The vast majority of 911 calls don’t involve fires; they involve ambulance requests.

“This move reflects the changes in the department,” said a paramedic commander who happens to be white.

“This is not about black and white, it’s about paramedics and firefighters, and it’s about time that our side of the department got some clout,” he said. “Trotter will give us that.”

The fire commissioner’s job is political and administrative, yes. But it is also about fighting fires and saving lives, not only of fire victims, but also the lives of firefighters under his command.

Daley has made the political decision that extensive fire command experience isn’t necessary for the top fire job. If you’ve got questions on that front, take it up with him.

The tragic and needless deaths at the 69 W. Washington St. fire may be attributed to a lack of command experience by some fire bosses at the scene. If Trotter had been commissioner then and it was found he had little if any fire command experience, he’d be the one forced to resign.

As a paramedic, Trotter will have to earn the respect of firefighters. As a black man, he has decided to push affirmative-action policies while dealing with a union opposed to racial preferences.

Trotter will need all his political skills to succeed. He’s been given a terribly tough job.

It’s all about fire, the political kind.
