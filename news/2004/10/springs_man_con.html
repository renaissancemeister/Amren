Springs Man Convicted Of Manslaughter In Mass. Death

Erin Emery, Denver Post, Oct. 16

A Colorado man studying for his master’s degree at Harvard University was convicted of manslaughter and sentenced to six to eight years in prison Thursday for stabbing a man to death in a fight in Cambridge, Mass.

Prosecutors had charged Alexander Pring-Wilson, 26, of Colorado Springs with murder in the slaying last year of Michael Colono, arguing that Pring-Wilson attacked the 18-year-old Colono for ridiculing him as he stumbled home drunk.

“Michael Colono made fun of the defendant, and it cost him his life,” prosecutor Adrienne Lynch said.

Pring-Wilson said he pulled a 4-inch folding knife and stabbed Colono in self-defense after Colono and his cousin brutally beat him.

The jury deliberated over five days before finding Pring-Wilson guilty of the lesser charge. Judge Regina Quinlan could have given him 20 years behind bars.

Ann Kaufman, who defended Pring-Wilson with co-counsel Rick Levinson, both from Colorado Springs, said she would appeal the case.

In Massachusetts, attorneys are not allowed by law to contact jurors, so Kaufman said she was unclear how the jury reached its verdict. After finding Pring-Wilson not guilty of first-degree murder and second-degree murder, the jury settled on voluntary manslaughter.

Under Massachusetts law, a person can be found guilty of manslaughter if a jury believes excessive force was used.

“If you couldn’t lawfully defend yourself under these circumstances, self-defense is effectively dead as a defense in the United States,” Kaufman said.

The attorney said that pervasive print and television coverage that dwelled on the different backgrounds of the two men — Pring-Wilson’s status as a Harvard student and son of two lawyers versus the victim’s history as a high school dropout who worked as a cook and earned his GED — tainted the case.

“The prosecutorial hype, the fantasy spin on the case, I think, fundamentally affected the fairness of the outcome,” Kaufman said. “And the painting of the victim as sort of a family guy instead of on probation for distribution of crack cocaine — it was fictionalized and sensationalized, and it was all a lie. And it worked very well for the prosecutor.”

George Simmons, who watched Pring- Wilson grow up in the house across the street, was pleased to hear that the jury had not convicted him of first-degree murder, which would have carried a sentence of life without parole.

However, he said, a sentence of six to eight years was “too much.”

Pring-Wilson began serving his sentence Thursday.

He was at Harvard studying for his master’s degree in Russian and Eurasian studies and planned to attend law school. Colono, a high school dropout, had earned his equivalency diploma and was working as a cook at a Boston hotel.

Pring-Wilson’s mother, Cynthia Pring, said she understood the Colono family’s pain. “I feel for them so strongly,” she said.

“My son feels for them so strongly.”
