17 — Uptown wrote at 12:18 AM on August 16:

Hate crimes are real bad jurisprudence.

First, because hate crime penalties are so severe, the state can coerce guilty pleas in doubtful cases by offering to dismiss the hate crime allegation. Prosecutorial overcharging is a common tactic at the federal level, and it frightens defendants to plea when they otherwise should not.

Second, hate crime allegations invite a criminal jury to focus on whether the defendant is a bad man. Assaying character, to decide the issue of guilt or innocence, undermines our traditional concern about unjustly convicting unpopular defendants.

Third, whether or not someone convicted of a hate crime was actually motivated by hate, is impervious to effective review on appeal.

Fourth, that the victim may have been vulnerable is a factor which can be considered on sentencing. There is no reason to make it a formal part of the jury trial.

The whole idea of a hate crime is a stopgap sop thrown to the public, to convince one and all that the criminal justice system is equal to certain of the emerging problems generated by racial hostility. It is not. Hate crime legislation is a bad idea when applied to white defendants, and it’s useless when applied to minority defendants, and we should say so, and move on.
