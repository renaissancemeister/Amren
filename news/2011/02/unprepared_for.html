Unprepared for Students’ Learning Gap

Tina Griego, Denver Post, February 17, 2011

The state Commission on Higher Education this month released its latest data on the still-pressing, ever-pressing problem of too many Colorado high school graduates entering college unable to do the regular coursework. Nearly a third of the Class of 2009 had to take at least one remedial class in math, writing or reading upon starting college that fall.

Four of the five traditional high schools with the highest percentage of students in need of remediation are in Denver. The other was Aurora Central. The Denver high schools were, in order of highest remediation needs: West, Abraham Lincoln, North and Montbello. In other words, basically the same schools that have been sending students to college unprepared for years now. And, no, this is not about bashing teachers, so you can put down the phone.

I just want to break this data down a bit. Any discussion of remediation requires it, and nothing is as sobering as seeing how the pieces lay out.

Nearly 91 percent of West High’s Class of 2009 entering a Colorado college that fall needed remediation. Nine of 10.

Wait. It gets worse.

West’s Class of 2009 had a 47 percent graduation rate. That is, of the 260 freshmen who entered the school in the fall of 2005, 122 students graduated four years later. Add to that group the students who entered West along the way or who took five years to graduate and the Class of 2009 ended up with 165 graduates.

Of those 165, 43 enrolled in a Colorado college or university in the fall of 2009.

Of those, 39 — the 91 percent — needed remedial classes. Nearly all needed help in every core subject: math, writing and reading.

Let me put it this way: Of the 2009 West college-bound graduates who enrolled in-state that fall, four were ready for college-level work.

This wasn’t an off-year for West. The percentage of its graduates needing remedial classes has been higher than any other Colorado high school for at least three years. It’s easy to single out West, but Lincoln, North and Montbello all have dismal graduation and remediation rates.

All are high-poverty schools where minorities are the majority.
