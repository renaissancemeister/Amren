Haiti, Sudan, Côte d’Ivoire: Who Cares?

Bret Stephens, Wall Street Journal, January 11, 2011

Once upon a time in West Africa, two kings named Acqua and Bell made a memorable request of British Prime Minister William Gladstone. “We are tired of governing the country ourselves,” they wrote in a letter dated Nov. 6, 1881. “Every dispute leads to war, and often to great loss of life, so we think it is the best thing to give up the country to you British men who no doubt will bring peace, civilization and Christianity in the country. Do for mercy’s sake please lay our request before the queen. . . . We are quite willing to abolish all our heathen customs.”

The kings’ offer (which Gladstone declined) makes for interesting reading as one postcolonial state — Sudan — votes this week to split in two, with uncertain consequences. Another state — Côte d’Ivoire — stands on a razor’s edge between outright dictatorship and civil war. And a third — Haiti, a de facto American colony from 1915 to 1934 — has proved unable to pick itself even inches off the ground since last year’s devastating earthquake. What, if anything, does it all mean?

It means that we’ve come full circle. It means that colonialism, for which the West has spent the past five decades in nonstop atonement, was far from the worst thing to befall much of the colonized world. It means, also, that some new version of colonialism may be the best thing that could happen to at least some countries in the postcolonial world.

Take Haiti. Haiti is no longer a colony of the West, but it has long been a ward of it. Even before the earthquake, remittances and foreign aid accounted for nearly 30% of its GDP. The country is known as the “Republic of NGOs,” since some 3,000 operate in it. What good they’ve done, considering the state the country has been in for decades, is an open question. Security, to the extent there is any, is provided by some 12,000 U.N. peacekeepers. {snip}

But last year’s fraudulent elections are a reminder that Haitians have been as ill-served by their democracy as by their periodic dictatorships. When “Baby Doc” Duvalier was overthrown in 1986, per capita GDP was $768. In 2009, on the eve of the quake, it was $519. Nor do the troubles end there: Criminality is rampant, and Haiti ranked 177th out of 179 on Transparency International’s 2008 corruption index. These are not the depredations of greedy foreign interlopers. This is the depravity of the locals.

{snip}

Côte d’Ivoire used to be one of those promising African states bucking the usual trends of the continent. But then per capita GDP plummeted by about 40% in the past 40 years. More recently, the country has seen a civil war between north and south and military intervention by French troops. Now its president, Laurent Gbagbo, refuses to concede an election the U.N. insists he lost to challenger Alassane Ouattara, a former International Monetary Fund official.

{snip} U.N. peacekeepers aren’t going to force him out. {snip} There’s the U.N. and all its failures explained in a nutshell.

{snip}

Postcolonial Africa has seen the future. As often as not, it looks like Zimbabwe. The West professes to “care” about countries like Haiti, Côte d’Ivoire and — at least for as long as George Clooney is in the area — south Sudan. But “care” at the level of simple emotion is little more than a cheap vanity. The colonialists of yore may often have been bigots, but they were also, just as often, doers. Their colonies were better places than the shipwrecked countries we have today.

{snip}

17 — Michael C. Scott wrote at 1:24 PM on January 13:

Africa probably could benefit from recolonization, but would the West? The only third-world colony that ever made it’s European owners much of a return was India. This is because local rulers at various levels were left in charge for the most part, so the place was largely self-governing (and certainly more peaceful than if English foreigners had been exclusively in charge). The Spanish certainly took a lot of gold out of their American colonies and used this to fund the Hapsburgs’ wars, but this was very much a mixed blessing, as the huge influx of gold merely cause massive price inflation in the food and manufactured goods needed for these wars. Finally can we be seriously expected to believe that France economically benefited from control of places like Chad or that Germany would never have risen to Great Power status without control of the Namibian desert or isolated islands in the Marshals and Carolines groups?

Colonial ownership carried an additional hazard; conflict. Not only were uprisings by the locals against colonial rule common (like the Indian Army Mutiny or the Anglo-Boer wars), but the locals were also in frequent conflict with each other (Zulus versus Xhosas, Zulus versus Boers etc, Burma’s attempt to invade India in 1824, etc.) This conflict forced the colonial powers to spend even more money than necessitated by ordinary policing and administrative work. Finally, it brought major powers into conflict with each other; Britain nearly went to war with Russia and France several times in the late 1800s and Britain and France did fight Russia from 1853-1856 over influence in what was a rapidly-collapsing Ottoman Empire. Japan fought China in 1895 for possession of Okinawa and Taiwan and then fought Russia over Korea in 1904-05, and France fought China in 1884-85 over control of Tonkin (now northern Vietnam). The example of colonial conflict best known by most Americans was the Spanish-American in 1898 over possession of Cuba, Puerto Rico and the Philippines. As much trouble as Spain was having with insurrections in these places, they certainly weren’t making a profit from owning them, and this leaves aside the devastating losses in warships and regrettable loss of life they suffered at Manila Bay and off Santiago.

Colonial possession also complicates domestic politics; the military coup in Portugal in 1974 was mainly the result of the Portuguese military’s distaste for the continued combat operations in Angola and Mozambique. This coup was brought off bloodlessly and free elections were held 18 months later, but the Algerian War saw the French army split during two attempted coups and this resulted in a civil war between pro-French hardliners in Algeria and Charles de Gaulle’s supporters; this included several attempts by rebel paratroops to invade France, including a successful occupation of Corsica by paratroopers based in Algeria. This conflict resulted in the collapse of the French Fourth Republic in 1958. It took some time for France’s troubles to subside even with the founding of the Fifth Republic; the Paris Massacre of 1961 saw some 200 peaceful demonstrators killed by police, and there were several assassination attempts made on de Gaulle’s life, including a machine gun ambush in 1962 in which 14 bullets hit de Gaulle’s Citroen, miraculously injuring nobody. Portugal’s and France’s political problems were clearly caused by colonialism.

My impression is that most of these colonies were acquired largely for reasons of national prestige, much in the manner space programs serve today. Given the multiple drawbacks to colony ownership, it is fair to state the West does not need them and never really did.
