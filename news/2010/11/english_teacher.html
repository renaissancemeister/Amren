English Teachers Look to Change Their Image

Robert Lee, Korea Herald, Nov. 17, 2010

One of Korea’s biggest foreign English teacher associations is taking an enlightened approach against the fight on mandatory HIV testing by correcting, rather than complaining about, the public’s image of English teachers as promiscuous party animals.

{snip}

National Communications Officer Rob Ouwehand of the Association of Teachers of English in Korea believes that the regulatory testing stems from both the fear of English teachers and HIV, both of which can be cured with knowledge.

In an interview with The Korea Herald, Ouwehand explained that rather than criticize the ministries and third party organizations involved, they look to themselves to polish the tarnished image of foreign English teachers.

“ATEK prefers taking a problem-solving approach to that kind of a question, where rather than complaining about some perceived injustice, why don’t we get to work on improving the reputation of English teachers in Korea, by going out into the community and doing good stuff.”

And knowledge of English teachers is exactly what the association plans to distribute through several positive means which includes building internal pressure, connecting with Korean English teachers and rebuilding the Korean public’s view of foreign teachers.

To reconnect with the public, Ouwehand believes they need to put themselves out there, swapping scary thoughts of English teachers with positive images.

The perceived reputation of foreign English teachers in Korea, fueled by the Anti-English Spectrum group and perpetuated by the media, had long been one of drinking, drug abuse, sexual promiscuity and disease.

It is speculated that the efforts of the vigilante group helped push the ministry’s institutionalization of the testing in 2007.

“It’s not so much English teachers, it’s the idea of English teachers,” he said.

“When English teachers go out into the community and volunteer, collect clothes for poor kids and volunteer English lessons at the orphanage nearby, than instead of being that kind of faceless, scary, English teacher, it humanizes us and by contributing to Korean society and saying we’re not here just to drink and party and take our money and go home. We’re part of Korean society, and we want to be responsible members and contributors to Korean society.”

{snip}

“The Korean government has already been working hard to promote the idea that Korea is a multicultural country and as they promote the idea that we can live together and understand each other, once again, that’s building the knowledge that will replace fear,” said Ouwehand.

{snip}

For the record, I disagree with the “huaghty” nature of the East Asian individual (haughtiness lies in East Asian governments), but I suppose everyone’s perceptions are different. I’ll focus on the issue in the article now, as its more relevant.

The majority of English teachers in Korea are not mistreated.The sources you give are a blog, which is not exactly reliable. The writer has some supporters, so I won’t be so foolish as to dismiss your point outright, but I do have some things to add.

“Recruiters, aren’t hiring older, experienced teachers because schools don’t want to pay them.”
That’s exactly what’s happening and I won’t even attempt to deny it. Even in Korea schools must run on a budget, and despite the attempt by the Government there to increase English education, its still a very secondary consideration in the minds of most principals at schools. Those hired are informed of their pay and are paid on time in the vast majority of cases (and I mean maybe one out of a thousand might be cheated, if even that). The vast majority of English teachers are young expats who want to experience another country without having to have money beforehand. English teaching is the ideal (temporary) solution. Once they’ve spent enough time in South Korea, they head home. I doubt there are many English teachers in Korea that are older than 40. I don’t know about Korea, but in Japan, they’re looked down upon as they’re seen to have no worthwhile activity to head home for, and are often shunned (even by other expats).
So these teachers aren’t “exploited” in the conventional sense, they’re just employed as they’re willing (not forced) to be paid less. Also, the blog that you cited is overwhelmingly pro-Korean in tone, and it’s quite obvious the (white) writer has enjoyed his time in South Korea (he even states he maintains a “connection” with the people), so it can’t be that bad to teach English there can it?

Moving back to the point…
Both Koreans and English teachers there must accept a portion of the blame. There have been crimes by English teachers in Korea, and there was a particularly notorious incident last year where a rape occurred with the teacher in question shared the files over the internet. On the korean side, you have a fanatic “Anti-English Spectrum” AES (ironically abbreviated in English) that has now started harassing English teachers after a gang of teachers disturbed passengers on a Seoul subway and posted the video on the expat site “English Spectrum”. So neither side is blameless.
But the Korean media is the most guilty party. Newspapers in Korea are heavily sensationalist and blow small incidents out of proportion, and agitate both sides of any issue (just look at the Korean media’s cries about the “epidemic of bullying” that’s sweeping tha nation, apparently inspired by Korean movies)

Better understanding is required from both sides. Finally where did everyone here get the idea that english teachers HAD to be white? Wasn’t there an article on amren a couple of months back about a black English teacher in Korea?
