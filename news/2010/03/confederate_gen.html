Confederate General Returns to Battlefield

David Zucchino, Los Angeles Times, March 21, 2010

The proud lieutenant commander of the Smithfield Light Infantry of the Sons of Confederate Veterans is John M. Booker, a burly retired veterinarian with a trove of Civil War books and an abiding fascination with all things Confederate.

Since 2006, Booker has devoted himself to erecting a statue of Joseph E. Johnston, the last Confederate general to mount an effective fight against Union forces. Johnston ultimately surrendered to Gen. William Tecumseh Sherman after the pivotal Battle of Bentonville, fought in March 1865 on a site a few miles from Booker’s white-columned Greek Revival home.

In an era when public enthusiasm for Confederate memorials has waned, Booker and the 24 fellow members of the Smithfield Light Infantry want to honor Johnston and the soldiers who fought and died under him. The camp — as such units are called — erects tombstones on Confederate graves, cleans up Confederate cemeteries and attends historical lectures.

{snip}

Booker’s camp refused to submit to the state’s vetting process for historical memorials. Instead, it erected the statue on a sliver of farmland donated by a transplanted New Yorker with an interest in Confederate lore. Booker spearheaded a fund drive that he said raised $94,500 for the statue.

The farmland is part of the 6,000-acre battlefield, but outside the state-run historic site.

The state responded by erecting a low fence between the statue and the official state-run visitor center. Next to the fence are three new signs noting the state property boundary just a few strides from the general’s likeness.

The statue depicts Johnston with his left arm raised. It’s a call for his troops to hold the line against Yankee forces, Booker said. “And,” he added, “to hold the line against political correctness.”

{snip}

Booker pointed out that the plaque at the foot of the statue did not require anyone’s approval. It reads: “Defender of the Southland to the End.”

Nor was approval required for the 5-by-8-foot Confederate battle flag the camp intends to fly on a 30-foot pole next to the statue. The flag is a tribute to the troops who fought under it, Booker said.

{snip}

To critics, commemorating the Confederacy represents an unhealthy obsession with a racist past. But to men like Booker, whose family has lived in North Carolina for generations, it builds bonds and cements friendships through studying and honoring local history.

{snip}

[Booker] avoids matters of race and slavery by simply saying that Confederate soldiers “fought and died for their cause — they believed the Southland had been invaded.”

To Petrarch:

Cotton had become a hot item in the international marketplace. The Southern states grew it economically by using slave labor; and were supplying the industrial fabric manufacturers from New England to Old England across the pond.

The Yankee blockade of Southern ports deprived these manufacturers of the raw cotton they needed to keep their mills running, though capture of stockpiles of un-exported Confederate cotton gained high profits for the Federal officers who could get these goods to the New England mills. England started experimenting with raising their own crops (in Turkey or India, I believe) which were inferior but kept their mills in operation. Neither plan worked without putting great stress on the viability of the industry.

After the Civil War, Northern profit seekers and established Southern plantation owners proved that, while still in demand, cotton could be grown profitably by tennant farmers without enslaving the laborers. But it remained a strained arrangement until technology provided steam-, and later, gasoline-powered tractors and mechanical harvesters.

At the time of The War, such technology was unthought of, and the South insisted on having the right to extend its profitable “peculiar institution” into the new western and southern territories opening up for expansion. Lincoln’s administration was committed to containing the slave system within its current boundaries.

I believe the “war over slavery” could easily have been averted had the fanatic abolitionists of the North kept their cool for a few more decades giving technology a chance to catch up and make the whole slavery argument moot.

That, however, wouldn’t necessarily have avoided conflict over interpretations of the Constitution, or the Federal government’s appetite for collecting Southern taxes to help build a more sophisticated Northern infrastructure; to name just a couple of the issues complicating the politics of the day. Heck, Civil War or not, states’ rights issues are STILL unresolved today.

But we do have a Union of States (with the Union having considerably more sovereignty than the states), and we have officially done away with slave labor. Unfortunately, Congress never saw fit to send the freed slaves off to their own colony in Africa or Central America. Lincoln had pushed for such a measure, and later, President Grant too. Preliminarily, arrangements had been negotiated; it WAS feasible, but since most blacks resided in the South, the Republicans in power didn’t see it as a priority. In fact, loyal black voters tended to keep the Black Radical Republican Yankee carpetbagger regime in power for a number of years after the war. Indignant White Southern resentment still seethes under the surface of polite resignation.
