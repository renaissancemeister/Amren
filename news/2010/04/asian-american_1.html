Asian-American Dems Take Offense at DCCC Hawaii Move

Ben Smith, The Politico, April 8, 2010

Asian-American Democrats are criticizing the Democratic Congressional Campaign Committee for favoring a white former congressman from Hawaii over the Asian-American State Senate President in a hotly contested special election to represent a majority-minority Hawaii district.

{snip}

“Imagine the disgruntled reaction were the DCCC to step into a contested special election in a predominantly African-American or Latino district,” Dutta [Gautam Dutta, the Asian American Action Fund executive director] pointedly added.

Viewing Case as the stronger candidate, the DCCC is reportedly quietly siding with former Rep. Ed Case over state Sen. Colleen Hanabusa in a nonpartisan election that also features a strong Republican candidate. {snip} Case, whose wife is Asian-American, has always been popular among Asian-American constituents, but the increasingly organized national Asian-American political class has long felt slighted by Democrats.

{snip}

Another prominent Asian-American operative said Asian-American Democrats see the move as “beyond an affront” and that there’s an effort to organize a boycott of the DCCC by Asian-American donors.

Allow me to make an important point here, regarding the key word “colonization”. It’s very misleading. The British never “colonized” India in the genuine sense of the word — meaning to populate it with “colonies” of themselves. They were content merely to rule it, not to settle there, and were happy to leave the inhabitants in place. There was no intent to displace them. In fact, they didn’t even “rule” it directly, but through local rulers. I have read, somewhere (I forget the exact statistic) that there were never any more than something like 15 or 20 thousand British in India at a time, administering a sub-continent (then consisting of many countries) of multiple millions. They “ruled” it though a sparse but highly efficient administration; but they never attempted, nor desired, to populate it (i.e. “colonize” it).

In India, the local rulers were left in place, the laws, the customs, the religions. Even the local armies. The British were only interested in controlling the commerce for the benefit of their empire, not (unlike the Spanish) in converting the populace, nor in re-populating the country (with themselves or anyone else). Thus, it was not a true “colony” of Britain in the precise sense [meaning an extension of Britain], but only a possession or an allied part of the empire. We don’t have sufficiently precise terminology to deal with this.

It was a very distinct situation from what developed in North America where they did indeed settle and take root, eventually, though not always by original intention. But even there, those settlements were confined and often planted outside of government approval/involvement (e.g. the Puritans who were defying England and trying to get away from it) or else acquired, already settled, from other powers (e.g. Niew Amsterdam, Quebec). The British came to North America with not the remotest intent to displace the American Indians and populate the continent. In fact, the British government often sided with the native Amerindians against the interests of the colonists — a major cause of the American Revolution. The colonists felt they had been abandoned, betrayed, by their own government and people back in London.
