Unlikely Group Charges Bias at University

Lisa W. Foderaro, New York Times, September 14, 2010

For more than 35 years, the City University of New York, one of the nation’s most diverse higher education systems, has quietly struggled with a minority group that says it has been passed over for jobs. {snip}

{snip}

Of all the universities and colleges that offer protections for minorities, CUNY appears to be the only one that has declared Italian-Americans an official affirmative action category in employment, promising special efforts to recruit, hire and promote them, according to national higher-education groups.

{snip}

Still, for some who work in higher education, the notion of protections for Italian-Americans — at a university where 70 percent of the 262,000 full-time students are black, Latino or Asian — has prompted some head-scratching.

7 — Anonymous wrote at 12:20 AM on September 17:

Just wanted to say a few words about CUNY. I graduated with a B.S. in Biology in 1981 from City College which is part of the CUNY system. The Sciences and Engineering Schools were excellent. Of course, it’s hard to dumb down science and math because 2+2 always equals 4 no matter how much one might wish it weren’t so.

However, what I really want to talk about is open admissions versus the old system. I got into City College just under the line before open admissions went into effect.

The old system worked like this. There were matriculated and non-matriculated students. If, for example, you wanted to take a course in Greek Mythology just because you liked it and you weren’t working toward a degree, you were a non-matric. At that time in the 1970’s you would pay $35 per credit which for a 3 credit course would be $105. If you were matriculated, you didn’t pay anything per course except for for a general fee which you paid each semester.

I started college when I was in my 30’s and here’s the beauty of the old system. If you wanted to get a degree but had dropped out of high school for some reason or, as in my case, had been out of school for a long time, you could start out as a non-matriculated student. If you completed 16 credits with a 3.0 average or 32 credits with a 2.0 average you were eligible to matriculate. Of course, you had to pay for your courses as a non-matric, but they were dirt cheap.

It’s a shame they did away with that old system. All you had to do was show that you were willing to study and pay a small amount of money and you could get into college and go on to pursue a degree. It was the best thing that ever happened to me.
