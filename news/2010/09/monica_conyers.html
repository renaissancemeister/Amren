Monica Conyers to Begin 3-Year Prison Term Today at W.Va. Camp Cupcake

Tresa Baldas, Detroit Free Press, September 10, 2010

{snip}

But come 2 o’clock today, Monica Conyers’ fight to stay out of prison should be officially over when she begins her 37-month prison sentence at the Alderson Federal Prison Camp in West Virginia.

The facility, nicknamed Camp Cupcake, is nestled among the bucolic foothills of the Allegheny Mountains in West Virginia along the Greenbrier River. It looks like a college campus — and is called that by locals — and has housed celebrity convicts such as Martha Stewart.

{snip}

Conyers pleaded guilty in 2009 to taking bribes to vote for Synagro Technologies’ $1.2-billion sludge disposal deal.

Her last-ditch effort to stay out of prison was shot down Thursday when a federal appellate court denied her request to remain free on bond until it considers her appeal.

Conyers is trying to withdraw her guilty plea. A federal judge has already refused her request. {snip}

{snip}

At sentencing March 10, she tried to withdraw her guilty plea, saying: “I just don’t feel that I should go to jail for something that I didn’t do.”

10 — Michael C. Scott wrote at 1:48 PM on September 16:

Short of a Presidential pardon or a successful appeal of her sentence, it is legally impossible for Conyers to serve less than 32.5 months out of the 37. Up to four months of the 32.5 can be at a halfway house, but those places aren’t much fun, either (the staff knows that the residents are almost home and don’t want to go back to prison, so they cheerfully bully them). Conyers can not appeal the conviction itself, since a guilty plea waves the right to do that. I unfortunately have quite a lot of first-hand knowledge as to the workings of federal sentencing law and BOP rules: the maximum time off for good behavior is one day for each seven actually served, and up to 10% of one’s sentence (maximum of six months but at least one month) will be at a halfway house.

She probably has three years of supervised release. This is not parole, because parole was abolished in the federal prison system in about 1986, so only inmates - like Leonard Peltier - who were sentenced before then (under the old law) get parole hearings. It’s basically supervised probation. One fills out a monthly report listing income and expenses, describes the vehicles one owns/drives, submits to random drug and alcohol tests and provides proof of employment or proof of attempts to obtain employment.
