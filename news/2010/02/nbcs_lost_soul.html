NBC’s Lost “Soul”

Jeane Macintosh and Christina Carrega, New York Post, February 5, 2010

A special NBC Black History Month lunch spread — featuring fried chicken, collard greens and black-eyed peas — sparked a commissary controversy yesterday, but the African-American chef who planned it doesn’t understand the fuss.

“All I wanted to do was make a meal that everyone would enjoy — and that I eat myself,” NBC cook Leslie Calhoun told The Post last night.

Calhoun’s proudly planned feast, which she began last year, hit a snag when Ahmir “Questlove” Thompson, the drummer for Jimmy Fallon’s “Late Night” show band, The Roots, shot a photo of the menu outside the network’s Rockefeller Center cafeteria and posted it on Twitter.

The menu quickly spread across the Internet and sparked a fury.

But Calhoun is confused.

“Questlove, who I serve every day and who enjoys my food, requested the neck bone [cooked in] the black-eyed peas and fried chicken, then got off the line, saying, ‘This is racist,’” she said.

“The next thing you know, people were taking pictures of the sign and asking all the other black people in the cafeteria if this was racist. They said that it wasn’t.”

{snip}

But Kevin Goldman, NBC’s vice president of communications, tweeted: “The sign in the NBCU cafeteria has been removed. We apologize for anyone who was offended by it.”

A disappointed Calhoun, who has worked at NBC for eight years, said she’s been begging for years to make special entrees in honor of Black History Month, and got her wish last year. The plan was to have one special meal every Thursday during February — although she said she’s nervous about next week.

{snip}


Leslie Calhoun.

These plants are not indigenous to Africa. They were carried here from Europe and their preparation was taught to African household servants.

“Predominantly a fall and winter vegetable, collard is often the only green planting to be found in the yard or garden of a rural home during these cool seasons. [Cabbage] is a cool-weather crop that can tolerate frost but not heat.” Botanically, collards are a form of cabbage, members of the colewort family, which grows across Europe and Asia. The word “collard” is a slurred version of “cole-wort”, an Anglo-Saxon word. [Literally, a “cabbage-plant” … cole (cabbage), as in ‘cole’ slaw.] Collard is a smooth-leaved form of Kale (kaal in Norwegian, kohl in German, col in Spanish) … a variety of non-heading cabbage which is close to the original wild plant. It is thought to have been brought to Britain and France by the Celts. As a result of thousands of years of selective breeding, , it has been developed into various forms (broccoli, kohlrabi, Brussels sprouts, cauliflower, bok choy, etc.). (Kohlrabi, meaning ‘cabbage-turnip’ was developed in 1554 by a German botanist. Germans favored colewort plants with fat stems; these became kohlrabi; Belgians selected plants with tightly packed leafy buds, and these became brussels sprouts.) A famous national dish of Portugal is Caldo Verde, a rich soup made of shredded collards. Highly nutritious, the cole family is rich in anti-oxidents for fighting cancer, also a good source of Vitamin C, potassium, and calcium, while having the fewest calories and least fat of any vegetable. These cole plants were staples eaten all over Europe and Asia before being brought to America. Cool weather plants, they were unknown to Africans. (And chickens, btw, originated in India.)

Indeed, many of the basic staples in the African diet today: peanuts, corn, peppers, plantains, bananas, tomatoes, potatoes, beans, cassava, sweet potatoes, etc. were brought to Africa by Europeans. Peanuts, for instance (a basic Afrian food today), were brought from Brazil by Portuguese slave ships. So the slave trade brought Africa benefits in some ways. Very few foods today considered “African” are actually of African origin — although there are a great many potentially valuable food plants which are native to Africa but remain still wild because, unlike Europeans and Asians, Africans did not cultivate and domesticate them into modern, edible forms suited for agriculture.

“collard: [n] - variety of kale having smooth leaves. Kale, in turn, is variety of cabbage in which the central leaves do not form a head, being nearly the original or wild form of the species. It is closer to wild cabbage than most domesticated forms. The species `Brassica’ contains a wide array of vegetables, including broccoli, cauliflower, and Brussels sprouts.”

Sorghum, okra, and yams ARE of African origin. Although okra first grew wild on the banks of the Nile, then was cultivated in Arabia and throughout the Mediterranean. It arrived (like peanuts) in West Africa via Portuguese ships from Brazil.
