Texas Cheerleader Who Refused to Cheer for Her Alleged Rapist Taking Case to Supreme Court: Lawyer

Nina Mandell, New York Daily News, December 23, 2010

A former high school cheerleader who claims she was kicked off the team when she refused to chant the name of her alleged rapist is taking her case to the Supreme Court.

The cheerleader is asking for the country’s highest court to reverse an appeals court ruling dismissing the suit and ordering her (and her parents) to reimburse tens of thousands of dollars in legal costs, the San Francisco Chronicle reported.

One is no more “cleared” by a grand jury than one is found “innocent” by a petit jury. Failure by the grand jury to return a true bill (indictment) means no more than the State failed to proffer sufficient evidence that this man committed a crime. The defendant’s side of the story is, for a grand jury, meaningless, because its decisions speak only to the State’s burden. He is not “cleared”; he is merely not charged, for the time being. Moreover, because a grand jury’s proceedings are not a final decision on the merits, double jeopardy protections don’t even arise. How, then, can it ever be said that the grand jury has “adjudicated” anything, one way or the other?
