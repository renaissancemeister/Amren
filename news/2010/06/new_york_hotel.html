“A child born in the US is not a “foreigner” at the time of birth […]”

This thesis leads to absurdity. For instance, a child born of a female soldier on an invaded territory would have to be considered “not a foreigner” as well. (One can build many other scenarios that lead to similar absurdities, for instance, a child born during international flight over a territory of the country in question, etc.) So, the thesis is false.

The only unproblematic convention here is a legal presumption that a newborn child inherits subject status of its biological mother; in particular, it is subject to the same jurisdiction that its mother is. Because if mother owe allegiance to, say, Mexico, her child inherits it (for instance, she can insist that she takes it back there). This would be very much in line with the “woman’s right to chose”.

But whatever convention is actually used, treating the clause “and subject to the jurisdiction thereof” as a meaningless figure of speech is pure nonsense.

9 — A Reader wrote at 5:47 PM on June 20:

Everyone within the boundaries of the United States is subject to US “law” - whether citizen or not.

There is nothing in the Constitution and its Amendments that would imply that “subject to the jurisdiction of the U.S.” and “subject to the U.S. law” are synonymous clauses. An example of group that was subject to the U.S. law but excluded from the “subject to the jurisdiction thereof” clause at the time of writing 14th Amendment were “Indians not taxed”. So the above quotation doesn’t settle the problem.

Several contemporary writings explained the “subject to […]” clause as an exclusion of persons owing allegiance to a foreign power or nation. Since Section 5 of 15th Amendment gives the Congress “power to enforce, by appropriate legislation”, all it takes to clear any ambiguity here once and for all is to pass a bill (as opposed to Constitutional Amendment) that would state that 1) “subject to the jurisdiction” means “not owing allegiance to any foreign country”, and that 2) in case of doubt, the burden of proof is on those who claim the birthright citizenship.
