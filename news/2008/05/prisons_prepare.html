Prisons Prepare to Integrate Cellmates

Tanya Schevitz, San Francisco Chronicle, May 27, 2008

San Quentin State Prison inmate Lexy Good is white, hangs out with whites on the prison exercise yard and must be careful not to associate with blacks and Latinos. No cards, no basketball outside the color lines.

Those are the unwritten inmate rules of prison life. People stick to their own race.

Good, who’s doing a short stretch for receiving stolen property, likes it that way.

“We segregate amongst ourselves because I’d rather hang out with white people, and blacks would rather hang out with people of their own race,” said Good, 33, of Walnut Creek. “Look at suburbia. Look at Oakland. Look at Beverly Hills. People in society self-segregate.”

Soon that may change in the prisons.

San Quentin and 30 or so other state penal facilities are gearing up to carry out a federal court mediation agreement for integrating double cells and ending the use of race as the sole determining factor in making cell assignments.

Men in California’s prisons have long been segregated in cells to quell racial tensions.

But Good, along with California’s other 155,700 male inmates, may soon be forced to live in a 4-by-9-foot cell with an inmate of a different race.

A 1995 lawsuit filed by a black California inmate, Garrison Johnson, said that the California Department of Corrections’ practice of segregating prisoners by race violated his rights. A 2005 ruling by the U.S. Supreme Court led to federal court mediation and the agreement that double cells would be desegregated.

While most inmates and correctional officials agree that it is a noble idea, many fear the worst.

“They should be thinking about what kind of war they are going to start,” said a San Quentin inmate who identified himself only as S. Styles, 36, of Vallejo. “It is like putting a cat and a dog in a cell together.”

Lt. Rudy Luna, assistant to the warden at San Quentin, said there is some concern among prison officials about the change because much of the violence is already based around racial gangs.

State mandate

{snip} [Luna said,] “I think we will have a spike in fighting because we have races that don’t get along. If it was up to us, we’d keep it the way it is. But it is a state mandate.”

Among the state’s male inmates, about 28.9 percent are black, 39.3 percent are Latino, 25.9 percent are white, and 5.9 percent are classified as other, according to figures from the state Department of Corrections.

“There are a lot of incidents in prison where you have a group of inmates going against another group of inmates,” said Terry Thornton, spokeswoman for the California Department of Corrections and Rehabilitation. “You have these groups aligned along race but it is about control. It is about criminal activities.”

As currently planned, the cell integration will begin July 1 as a pilot project at two prisons — Mule Creek State Prison in Ione (Amador County) and the Sierra Conservation Center in Jamestown (Tuolumne County). Next year, the plan calls for integration to begin at other prisons.

Interviewing inmates

In carrying out the plan, prison officials will interview and evaluate each inmate. Department of Corrections officials know some inmates cannot be placed with inmates of other races. {snip}

Inmate David Johnson said that all the races sit together peacefully in the prison church and they work together with few problems. But he wouldn’t socialize with inmates of another race outside of those settings where he is forced to mingle.

Loyal to his race

“Prison politics” dictate that he stay loyal to his race, Johnson said. And the repercussions for a violation are swift and severe.

“You would be taken care of in some way. You could get stabbed or worse,” said Johnson, 38, of San Diego. “Whether you agree with the (unwritten) rules or not, you have to follow them.”

{snip}

{snip}

However, experts say it can work. The Texas prison system integrated its cells in the early 1990s and eventually saw a decline in racial tensions, said Professor Jim Marquart, chair of the criminology department at the University of Texas at Dallas, who studied the transition and is advising California during its process.

All they did for us in federal prison was require high-school dropouts to finish a GED, and instruction was mediocre. One is in prison to be warehoused until that part of one’s life that the court has decided to remove is gone. That’s all. It will certainly change an individual. One finds out who one’s real friends are. While I was locked up, the fellow I thought was my best friend was housesitting for me. He stole everything that wasn’t nailed down, and broke everything that was. He was a TSA agent. Now he’s doing three-to-life with mandatory life on parole for molesting kids. A former friend whom I had thought would never speak to me again wrote to me twice a month. Old college friends sent me dozens of books, which I shared around the unit.

Race relations were fairly good where I was locked up. The shot-callers (bosses) simply didn’t allow interracial violence, so race relations were guarded and careful, but cordial. One of my gigs was doing laundry. We were each expected to do our own laundry, but some inmates were lazy. Others had never moved out of their mother’s homes before being arrested and thought they had to separate whites from colors even in the drier. It was four postage stamps or eight cigarettes a load, washed, dried and folded. Some of my cusomers were black. One just paid me to fold the laundry he did himself, because he said I folded it like his mom used to. One stamp a load. Another regular asked me my last evening in stir whether I could do his laundry the next day. When I told him I was getting out, he looked annoyed at first, and then just grinned, shook my hand, and said he hoped he’d never see me again. I still do all the laundry at home.

The food started out pretty good - basically military chow, but rapidly got worse as the budget crisis deepened. Issue clothing was one pair of steel-toed work shoes, and three each pairs of socks, boxer shorts, t-shirts, uniform trousers and uniform shirts. I was allowed to choose two long-sleeved shirts and one short-sleeve shirt. Coats were issued during the winter. I bought my own sweatpants and sweatshirt and one pair of sneakers during those three years. The sneakers eventually fell apart, despite my repeatedly sewing the uppers back onto the soles with dental floss (I did a lot of sewing work for other inmates, too.) By the time my atheletic shoes gave up, I had scrounged another pair. I used to stay up late in the dorm, and hit the “free stuff” shelf, late nights, mainly for the books that ended up there, but lots of shoes did as well. One of my students got a nearly new pair of shoes in exactly his size, after I found them there. I also gave him my alarm clock when I was released. It is considered good prison manners to give away things one won’t need on the outside, just before release.

I had contrived to be issued a new pair of work shoes, and never told my mother that she could send me release clothes, so I wore the new shoes out the gate (basically US navy “low quarters”) and they gave me a pair of “Rustler” blue jeans (now cutoffs, five years later) and a green Haynes pocket-T-shirt. I kept the sweats handy, because my last month would be in a halfway house and I wanted something to sleep in. The feds let me keep the socks and boxer shorts. Those low-quarters are now a pair of dress shoes for occasions when my black Oxford wingtips are too good. Most of the socks are gone, five years later. The boxers are spare clothes at my mother’s house.

The things I missed the most in prison didn’t include my home. I’d been away to college before, and lived in the dorms. Prison in Ft. Worth was like being at a really bad state college. I’d lived in a one-room flat in Australia, and THEN I missed my home. I most missed being able to get into my car and drive to the store to buy anything I wanted. I missed quiet, until I discovered that by staying up late in the smoking room, I could be alone, drink coffee, smoke a cigarette, and write. Yes, I smoked while I was in the feds. I quit the evening before I walked out the gate. I very much liked the combination of a cigarette and a cup of strong coffee, but nicotine never had much of a hold on me. The only cigarettes I really liked were the first and the last each day. I went through two packs a week.

I did three years of federal Supervised Release in reasonable peace, the city police here still think I’m OK. When I returned home, my P.O. walked around the neighborhood warning everyone I was back. Some of the old-timers knocked on my door to say “welcome back”, but I still used to get the mail at night, just so nobody would see me.

Married now, with a daughter, I *still* can’t drive to the store and buy anything I want, but it’s a good thing now.

Don’t harsh on the inmates too much. Some of them are evil, but others are stupid, or merely unwise.

I was in there once to do some welding, and it smelled like dying people. Not good. “Ft. Worth” was for elderly and handicapped inmates, so it had wheelchair ramps and the like. My four-story building had the other three dorms. One floor - “San Antonio” was for drug offenders who were doing a 500-hour drug class as part of a sentence-reduction program. “Austin” dorm, where I was was for healthy inmates who could work to maintain the complex. This was on the top two floors and they were locked apart only during 4:00 PM count time. The bottom floor of our building was “Houston” dorm, and these were guys like us who had messed up in one way or another, so they didn’t have a pool table or as many TVs.

The other guys varied in background quite a bit. One of my friends, a Jicarilla Apache, had been in for murder and mutiny since 1982. He had killed two FBI agents on the reservation while deserting his post as an active-duty soldier. His good behavior had allowed him to work down to a “low” cusody level. Another friend had burned a cross on the lawn of a crack house, and since the state wouldn’t prosecute him, the feds took the case as a civil rights violation. My file says I taught a guy to read, but it isn’t so. My bunkmate could already read at a “Tip & Mitten” level, and I merely brought him up to “Aviation Week & Space Technology”. He was built approximately like Frankenstein’s monster, and had been caught driving to his meth cook’s house with too much Sudafed (more than 500 pills). This is a six-month federal misdemeanor. He was also caught with illegal fireworks, a misdemeanor in Texas, but the feds called it a ten-year explosives enhacement, so he got 10.5 years on a pair of misdemeanors. His public defender did a good job, and got him the max. The fact that he was sent to a low and not a medium proves that he was not really considered dangerous.

Most of us were non-violent offenders who were not camp-eligible.

The only serious inmate-on-inmate violence I recall was when a Puerto Rican called a Cuban a “snitch”. The Cuban stabbed him right away. That’s also good prison manners: never snitch and never tolerate being called one.

To someone about to enter the system, I would offer the following advice: stay away from drugs, gambling, homosexuals, and blacks. Some of the blacks are OK, but what about their friends? Make a few close friends and stick with them like glue. Wahr dich, wehr dich, wach auf.
