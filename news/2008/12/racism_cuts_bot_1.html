It’s easy to organize at the grass-roots level in the British Isles because they’re small geographically and in many areas the population is very dense, making it easy to canvass. Many British homes, if not most, have mail slots where a leafleter can drop literature through; this is rare in the US, outside of maybe the Northeast.

Legally, the US presents a number of challenges. Perhaps the greatest irony is that the very structure of the American political system inhibits that very freedom of expression that it was founded to allow. A political party has to comply with 50 distinct sets of ballot-access and campaign finance laws, plus those for the District of Columbia, plus those for federal elections (the Federal Election Commission / FEC). The Democrats and Republicans long ago rigged the system to force third parties to spend their meager resources just trying to get on the ballot, leaving them virtually nothing for fighting a campaign once they do get on.

Many of the larger third parties, such as the Constitution, Green and Libertarian, focus on presidential politics because often that is the easiest way to get onto the ballot. (In Arkansas, for example, placing presidential electors on the ballot requires only 1,000 signatures, whereas getting on as a party requires 10,000.)

In my opinion, third parties in America should be built state by state. Unfortunately, Americans tend not to think of themselves as citizens of their state, but as citizens of their nation. Building a party from the grass roots up just takes too long and requires too much effort to be poured into one area.

If you want to see an American BNP, then demand fair and reasonable ballot-access laws in your state. All that should be required for a political party to be on your state’s ballot is that it be organized (as is the case in Vermont and Mississippi). Signature quotas, canvassing periods and other such requirements exist merely to keep third parties off the ballot so they cannot challenge the Democrat / Republican stranglehold. Demand an end to campaign-contribution limits, which penalize small parties with few potential contributors. Demand that all candidates in a race be allowed to attend any debates that are scheduled; or, in the case of a presidential contest, that all candidates who could theoretically obtain at least 270 electoral votes be included.

Politics as usual will continue unless you make ballot access a major issue where you live.
