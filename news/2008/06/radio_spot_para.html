Just ask anyone who lived in a formerly all White neighborhood in any city what it was like before diversity struck and after.
Let’s take the North Penn (sometimes known as Swampoodle) section of Philadelphia for example (the following limits are rough estimates)bounded by Allgeheny Ave. on the north and by Lehigh Avenue on the south and by Broad street on the east and by 33rd. street on the west.
Before:
1. People really could leave doors unlocked at night.
2. People really did sleep on their front porch gliders or chaise lounges on hot nights in the summer before air conditioning became commonplace.
3. If as a kid you did something “inappropriate” several blocks away your parents were waiting in ambush for you when you got home.
4. Everyone knew his neighbors and looked out for them.
5. Serious disputes between neighbors resulted in mutual shunning rather than in assault and murder. My family didn’t speak to the family two houses away but we kids still played together all the time.
6. Everyone with whom I was acquainted could identify all their relatives and family lines were clearly drawn.
7. Although many people owned firearms, the peace of evening was never disturbed by the sound of gunfire. Shotguns were sold at the local hardware store.
8. WE had block parties in the summer where everyone contributed food and entertainment.
9. Although we were far from perfect none of us was ever afraid to walk the streets at night.
10. The only fire I remember was one that started in my neighbor’s kitchen due to carelessness with cooking oil. Neighbors rushed to her aid and extinguished it within minutes with little resultant damage.

After
1. Frequent fires.
2. Assaults, armed robberies and B & E.
3. Rapes and attempted rapes.
4. Gunshots.
5. Doors being locked and bars being installed on basement windows.
5. Sale signs springing up everywhere.
6. Eventual loss of property value, the loss of the unique character and the eventual death of the neighborhood.

We still have reunions every few years and talk about how we miss the “old neighborhood” and how “different” it is where we now live.
