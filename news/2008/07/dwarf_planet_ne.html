Reuters, July 20, 2008

A dwarf planet orbiting beyond Neptune has been designated the third plutoid in the solar system and given the name Makemake, the International Astronomical Union said on Saturday.

The red methane-covered dwarf planet formerly known as 2005 FY9 or “Easterbunny” is named after a Polynesian creator of humanity and god of fertility.

{snip}

Makemake is just slightly smaller and dimmer than Pluto and was discovered in 2005.

“The orbit is not particularly strange, but the object itself is big, probably about two-thirds the size of Pluto,” said Mike Brown of the California Institute of Technology, who discovered and named Makemake (pronounced MAH-keh MAH-keh).

{snip}

Brown said the name came to him when he was looking for a mythological god and thought of the South Pacific’s Easter Island. Makemake was the chief god among people who settled the island.

There are a lot of things like that out there - to many to name after Roman dieties. Google “Trans-Neptunian Objects” for a singular lesson in just how many large “snowballs” orbit beyond Neptune.

Our solar system really has two belts. The first known was the asteroid belt between Mars and Jupiter. Ceres was the first of these discovered, and was first thought to be a small, lone planet, about the size of Texas. Other objects were soon discovered, and the nature of the asteroid belt became clear.

The Kuiper Belt beyond Neptune is much the same, with Pluto much in the same category as Ceres - once thought to be a planet, but now known to be a major member of a very large association of similar individuals. Unlike the rocky asteroid belt, in the Kuiper Belt things like ice and frozen gases predominate. Since many objects are in eccentric orbits and/or strongly inclined to the plane of the ecliptic, they are difficult to detect. Since we hae discovered quite a lot of them, this must mean there are a huge number of objects.

Three major populations of Kuiper Belt objects are known: “Plutinos”. These are objects that like Pluto, share a 3:2 orbital resonance with Neptune. “Two-One-Ohs”, which share a 2:1 orbital resonance with Neptune (these are farther out), and “Scattered Disc Objects”, which orbit yet farther out. A peculiar “Extended Scattered Disc Object” named Sedna is also known. Sedna’s very eccentric orbit takes it between the Kuiper Belt partway to the Oort Cloud.

It’s really a stretch to equate the naming of a minor and insignificant dwarf planet, of which there may be hundreds yet to be discovered, to some supposed insult to Western culture. The reason that the object got the name it did, is because it was nicknamed Easter Bunny by the astronomer who discovered it, because he found it on Easter Sunday. It’s common for astronomers to give celestial objects nicknames as placeholders, because their discoveries must be verified by others before it can receive it’s official name. When the time came to name it, he wanted to keep the Easter association, so he named it Makemake, a deity of Easter Island. This is because the tradition of naming planets after deities.
