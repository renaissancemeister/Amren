I’m not buying the idea that fast food is inherently fattening. The fat and salt content depends on what fast food one is talking about. The first job I was able to get after my release from federal prison was at Subway; I took it just to keep my P.O. happy, and not p.o’d. I got a free meal each shift I worked, so I ate Subway sandwiches five or six days a week for most of a year. I didn’t gain any weight. My favorites were the chicken pizzaiola (chicken, pepperoni and marinara sauce) and chicken-bacon-swiss melt, loaded with vegetables in each case. We had pastrami in the store, too, so sometimes I would bring Thousand Island dressing and sauerkraut from home and make myself a Reuben. I brought unsweetened iced tea from home to drink.

What I was conspicuosly not doing was eating MacDonald’s Big Macs, in which the beef is fried, the buns are sweetened with sugar to make them more appealing to kids, and chasing them with French fries deep-fried in more beef tallow and a large sugary soft drink. In fact, the reason I applied at Subway in the first place is because when I walked into the store, I could tell by the smells present that they were not frying anything.

Eating fast food is a collection of choices. One can chose which restaurant one goes to, and once one is inside, one gets to chose what to order. In fact, one also gets to chose whether to eat lunch out at all during the workweek. My wife does not. She makes salads at home and brings them to work in Tupperware, usually with a packet of instant miso soup. Alternately, she makes a sandwich in pita bread and takes that, in both cases with iced tea I brew at home.

If in fact “nobody sells a salad” in that area, I suspect it is the result of restaurant owners tailoring their selections to what customers are willing to buy. With an eye on the bottom line, no franchise owner really wants to write off a lot of wilted lettuce and spoiled tomatoes every week. That said, is there something actively preventing the residents from *making their own salads*? Even if you’re in a hurry, it’s not complicated. Grab some “garden salad” mix out of a bag in the refrigerator. Supermarkets sell a variety of these. Throw it in Tupperware, along with some tomato, onion, cucumber slices, almond accents and sunflower seeds or croutons, and the dressing of your choice. This might take two or three whole minutes out of someone’s busy day, either the previous evening, or in the morning before work, and it costs a lot less than going out to eat.
