				<h1>BP Explorers collect trash left by illegal immigrants in the Huachucas</h1>

<h3></h3>
    	
		<p class="credits">
		By Derek Jordan<br/>Herald/Review<br>
		Published/Last Modified on Sunday, Mar 29, 2009 - 02:15:13 am MST
    	</p>

<p>STUMP CANYON &mdash; A group of teenagers hiked out here on Saturday morning to collect trash left over from illegal immigrant traffic in the area.<br/><br/>The teens were members of a U.S. Border Patrol program out of the Naco station that seeks to teach skills and offer basic training to teenagers who are interested in law enforcement.<br/><br/>One day a week, the U.S. Border Patrol&rsquo;s Naco Station Explorer Program teaches teenagers everything from proper handcuffing and arresting techniques to how to handle firearms and clear a building, said William Schaeck, Border Patrol agent and lead adviser of the program.<br/><br/>&ldquo;It&rsquo;s pretty much all encompassing,&rdquo; Schaeck said of the year-round program.</p>

		<p><br/>
		Explorers are required to put in 40 hours of community service before they can graduate from the 12-week training period. The garbage collection was a good opportunity to pare down a few of those hours, he said.<br/><br/>&ldquo;It&rsquo;s not that bad,&rdquo; said 15-year-old Marco Pace. Only two weeks into the program, Pace said he&rsquo;s already enjoying it.<br/><br/>&ldquo;It&rsquo;s pretty fun,&rdquo; he said while stuffing an old backpack into one of the large, 42-gallon black garbage bags each of the eight explorer members was carrying. &ldquo;I wanted to join to see just what the Border Patrol does.&rdquo;<br/><br/>Now, he said, he&rsquo;s considering a career with the Border Patrol, law enforcement or the military.<br/><br/>For 16-year-old Ian Lea, enrollment in the program was a stepping stone to help further his education.<br/><br/>&ldquo;Anything like this will definitely look good on any college application,&rdquo; Lea said. &ldquo;If they see this, then they know you can&rsquo;t be too bad of a person.&rdquo;<br/><br/>Bisbee resident Craig Kost, 19, has been in the Explorer program for about a year. He originally joined up for the structure the program offered.<br/><br/>&ldquo;I wanted to be more disciplined and respectful,&rdquo; Kost said.<br/><br/>He&rsquo;s now thinking about a career with the Border Patrol.<br/><br/>As the group moved deeper and deeper into the brush following the wash, sparse traces of litter &mdash; plastic bottles, jackets, empty plastic bags &mdash; gave way to hundreds of discarded sweaters, shoes and other articles of clothing.<br/><br/>Border Patrol Agent Nora Cosgrove, who guided the Explorers along with Schaeck, said the group filled 27 large trash bags on the 3-mile trip in about three hours before even reaching the large deposit of discarded garbage.<br/><br/>Already faced with at least three full bags to carry per person the 3 miles back to the vehicles, the group wasn&rsquo;t able to tackle the &ldquo;motherlode&rdquo; of garbage.<br/><br/>However, now that the location has been established, agents will continue efforts to clean the area up, Cosgrove said.<br/><br/>&ldquo;We do a lot of these throughout the year,&rdquo; she said, &ldquo;so we may get back up there at some point.&rdquo;<br/><br/>In the meantime, the Douglas Station&rsquo;s Explorer Program members have been notified of the mess and they may lead a similar group out to the area, she said.<br/><br/>Herald/Review reporter Derek Jordan can be reached at 515-4680 or by e-mail at <a href="mailto:derek.jordan@svherald.com">derek.jordan@svherald.com</a>.</p>
