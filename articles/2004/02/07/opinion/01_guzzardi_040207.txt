<h2>U.S. not speeding toward 'ethnic ambiguity'</h2>
<h4 id="subHead"></h4>
<div class="lnsSans lnsMediumText">

</div>
<span class="idxTimestamp">Updated: Saturday, February 7, 2004 8:22 AM PST</span><br/>
<div class="lnsSans lnsSmallText">
<a href="#feedback">Comments (<span class="simpleblog-count">No comments posted.</span>)</a>
</div>
<p>
About a year ago, I noticed that during television ads I could no longer identify the models' ethnicity. Up until that time, politically correct ads went to great pains to have an equal distribution of males and females who were black, white, Asian and Hispanic.<br/><br/>I called the models "blended people." But the official term, I recently learned, is "ethnically ambiguous."<img src="http://lodinews.com/columnist_mugs/guzzardi_joe.jpg" width="180" height="108" align="right" alt="Joe Guzzardi"><br/><br/>In a Dec. 28 New York Times column titled "Generation E.A.: Ethnically Ambiguous," Ruth La Ferla quoted Ron Berger, chief executive of New York's advertising and trend research company Euro RSCG MVBMS Partners: "Today what's ethnically neutral, diverse or ambiguous has tremendous appeal both in the mainstream and at the high end of the marketplace. What is perceived as good, desirable and successful is often a face whose heritage is hard to pin down."<br/><br/>Advertisers make no bones about it. They purposely seek out models with indeterminate features for their shoots. An example is Indian model Ujjwala, the new face of the exclusive YSL Beauty Cosmetics. Her agent, Ivan Bart, says, "You look at her and you begin to play a guessing game. Is she Mexican, Spanish, Russian? The fact that you can't be sure is part of her seductiveness."<br/><br/>Two high-profile sport heroes are EAs: golf champion Tiger Woods -- father black and mother Thai -- and New York Yankee shortstop Derek Jeter -- black and Caucasian.<br/><br/>Among the 25-year-old and younger members of Generation Y, the most diverse population in America's history, multiracial is "in." Demographers point to sustained high levels of immigration and intermarriage between different groups of those immigrants as the driving force behind an increasingly multiracial America.<br/><br/>La Ferla quoted Gregory Spencer, the Census Bureau population projections chief: "The younger the age group, the more diverse the population."<br/><br/>When the results of Census 2000 were first published, much was written about the 7 million Americans who identified themselves as members of more than one race. These individuals took advantage of the opportunity the Census provided to check more than one category for race.<br/><br/>Another 14 million Hispanics skipped over boxes for black or white, choosing instead "some other race."<br/><br/>Certain geographic regions are more apt to have significant numbers of ethnically ambiguous people. The obvious places that pop into mind are Los Angeles, San Francisco, New York and Miami.<br/><br/>Particular professions have a highly visible EA representation. In the world of fashion, film and music we look at Jennifer Lopez, Shakira, Christina Aguillera and Vin Diesel.<br/><br/>For advertisers, ethnic ambiguity is a bonanza. H &amp; M, a Stockholm-based marketer of cheap but cool clothing with stores in 18 countries use "racial indeterminate" models only. Said a spokesman, "For us the models must be inspiring and attractive but at the same time neutral."<br/><br/>So is America on a run-away freight train toward ethnic ambiguity?<br/><br/>The sub-rosa message in La Ferla's article is that ethnic ambiguity -- through continued immigration and intermarriage -- will make race and culture pass&eacute;. And since the models and athletes chosen for the piece are strikingly beautiful and skilled, the conclusion we are urged to draw is that ethnic ambiguity will create the ultimate melting pot.<br/><br/>This one-day may well happen. And perhaps we are indeed looking into America's future on our television sets.<br/><br/>But to find out just how fast the U.S. might turn into an ethnically ambiguous country, I contacted one of the nation's experts on population and intermarriage, UPI National Correspondent Steve Sailer.<br/><br/>Sailer deals in the concrete world of census data -- not Hollywood or Seventh Avenue. Here, from his UPI story titled "Interracial Marriage Gender Gap Grows," is how he parsed the Census 2000 figures:<br/><br/>&bull; Only 3.5 percent of married non-Hispanic whites intermarry.<br/><br/>&bull; Only 6 percent of black husbands and 2 percent of black wives are in interracial marriages<br/><br/>&bull; Slightly under 18 percent of Hispanic wives have non-Hispanic husbands while slightly over 15 percent of Hispanic husbands have non-Hispanic wives.<br/><br/>&bull; About 18 percent of Asian wives have white husbands but only 7 percent of Asian husbands have white wives.<br/><br/>As for the immigration portion of the ethnic ambiguity equation, Sailer found that recent immigrants are the least likely of groups to intermarry.<br/><br/>Quoting from a Public Policy Institute of California study by Sonya M. Tafoya titled "Mixed Race and Ethnicity," Sailer writes:<br/><br/>"Explanations include the fact that the foreign-born may be married at the time of immigration, they might be more likely to live in ethnic enclaves, they might be more closely tied to a culture that resists out-marriage, or they might encounter language barriers."<br/><br/>Despite the buzz from the image-makers, progress toward total ethnic ambiguity, whatever opinion you may have of it, is moving slower than it appears.<br/><br/><div class="divider"></div><span id="columnist-tags">Joe Guzzardi, an instructor at the Lodi Adult School, has been writing a weekly opinion column since 1988. He can be reached via <a href="mailto:joaquin@lodinet.com">e-mail</a>.</span> 
</p>
