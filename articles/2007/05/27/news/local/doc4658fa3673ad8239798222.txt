</div>
		<p id="fontSize" class="default"><font face="Arial">John Schinckel and Ben Fuhendorf would have been proud on Saturday as descendants of German immigrants attended the second annual Founder&rsquo;s Day ceremony, this time held at Fairmount Cemetery in Davenport.<br/><br/>The first cousins arrived here in 1908 from Germany but rest in Fairmount now.<br/><br/>&ldquo;This is to honor the German immigrants who are buried here and who came to establish the area,&rdquo; said Noreen Steenbock, secretary of the Schuetzenpark Gilde.<br/><br/>John Schinckel&rsquo;s son, Jack, is the president of the American Schleswig-Holstein Heritage Society.<br/><br/>Many of the Germans in the area came from a part of northern Germany known as Schleswig-Holstein.<br/><br/>&ldquo;There was a huge wave of immigration after the failed revolution of 1848,&rdquo; said Kody Darnall, president of the Schuetzenpark Gilde.<br/><br/>In the mausoleum, such names as Sonntag, Schreiber, Grosskopf and Beitz provided proof.<br/><br/>And despite the Saturday rain and intermittent downpours, about 80 people attended. The Bix Beiderbecke Youth Jazz Band, directed by Nikki Whittaker of Knox College, played a few tunes, including one of Bix&rsquo;s own, &ldquo;In a Mist.&rdquo; The ties were obvious, as Bix&rsquo;s paternal grandparents were born in Germany.&nbsp;<br/><br/>Carol Schaefer said the event was to remind us &ldquo;what we owe to those who came before us,&rdquo; both German immigrants and others.<br/><br/>&ldquo;Think of those who sailed the oceans, left their families &hellip; to start a new life. They made a special effort,&rdquo; he said, to find the freedoms in the United States.<br/><br/>Historian Scott Christiansen spoke of such immigrants as architect Frederick George Clausen, who designed the Peterson (Redstone) Building, Central High School, churches and other buildings. He said his own great-great-grandfather J&uuml;rgen Peter Ankerson had a vineyard that overlooked the cemetery.&nbsp;<br/><br/>Christiansen then read the words of Henry Lau, spoken a 100 years ago at the fifth annual gathering of the German American Society of Scott County. &ldquo;Oppressed and troubled they left,&rdquo; he read. They were searching for &ldquo;a better existence.&rdquo; They traveled here by riverboat and by land and &ldquo;before them lay primeval forest and prairie.&rdquo;<br/><br/>North Scott High School student Hailee Gehrls sang three patriotic songs, and alderman Ian Frink spoke a few words. A color guard from the American Legion Davenport Post 26 provided a gun salute outside, and John Adams played taps.<br/><br/><font face="Arial">The city desk can be contacted at (563) 383-2450 or <a href="mailto:newsroom@qctimes.com">newsroom@qctimes.com</a>. </font><br/><br/></font></p>
