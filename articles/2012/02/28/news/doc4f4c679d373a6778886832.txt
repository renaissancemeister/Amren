                            <p><em>Eastpointe district will open borders to K-8 students within Macomb County</em><br/><br/>Faced with the grim prospect of payless paydays this summer, the East Detroit Board of Education voted 4-3 Monday night to adopt a Schools of Choice plan for K-8 students in Macomb County, but did not include opening the borders to neighboring counties.<br/><br/>School administrators say under a best-case scenario, the district can attract up to 600 students that would bring in about $4 million in additional state aid revenue. But skeptics doubt those numbers are possible without including students from neighboring Wayne County, including Detroit and Harper Woods.<br/><br/>&ldquo;I don&rsquo;t think we can pull that many kids from the county because they keep going to greener pastures northward,&rdquo; said board President Craig Wodecki, who voted against the plan.<br/><br/>Board members Jon Gruenberg, Jon G. Gruenberg, Margaret Podsiadlik and Deena Trocino voted in favor of the plan, while Wodecki, and Paul Seibert, and Craig Brozowski were opposed.<br/><br/>The Eastpointe-based school district has long opposed approving a Schools of Choice program, which allows students from other districts within the Macomb Intermediate School District borders to sign up with other districts and take along the per-pupil state aid revenue that goes with each.<br/><br/>In fact, until Monday, East Detroit was the only one of the 21 school communities in Macomb County that didn&rsquo;t have some form of Schools of Choice. All of the districts allow inter-county students, but only six also accept students from outside counties &mdash; Armada, Clintondale, Fitzgerald, Mount Clemens, Richmond and Romeo.<br/><br/>The move is an urgent one for East Detroit, which has lost at least 1,400 students over the past four years and has $4.2 million less in operating revenue than last year, due to decreases in state and federal assistance, along with the loss of the student population.<br/><br/>Schools of Choice &ldquo;approval is critical to the survival of this district,&rdquo; said Superintendent Joanne Lelekatch.<br/><br/>Prior to voting on the controversial program, the school board heard a report from Michael Dixon, the district&rsquo;s contracted finance director, who painted a gloomier picture. He said the district has reached its limit on borrowing money to the point where half of the $35 million budget is aimed at paying off loans and the budget deficit.   <span class="continue"><a href="?viewmode=2">Continued...</a></span></p>

												<div id="paragraphs2"><p>The district&rsquo;s budget is now pegged at $8.4 million and could balloon to $11.6 million in 2013. Under state law, school districts must submit a balanced budget each year or render a deficit reduction plan to show the state how it plans to address its red ink.<br/><br/>More problematic is the rapid decrease in student population, which is now at 3,500. Enrollment could plunge to 1,980 within five years, which means East Detroit Public Schools will no longer have the financial resources to be in business.<br/><br/>Dixon said it appears East Detroit may have a hard time meeting its payroll in April if a loan isn&rsquo;t secured. If a loan is landed, payless paydays will be likely by August unless new revenue is found.<br/><br/>&ldquo;We are going to be at a waterloo sooner than we want to be,&rdquo; Dixon said. &ldquo;The only way to add state aid is to add kids. We need more kids and we need them right away.&rdquo;<br/><br/>School officials have said residents in the district oppose allowing in youngsters from Detroit because they are apprehensive of behavioral problems.
